📌 Level 03 · Valuation · L3.04 — DCF — Discounted Cash Flow

The DCF is the only valuation methodology that does not depend on market prices or comparable transactions. It asks: based purely on this company's own cash-generating ability, what is it worth today? The answer is powerful — and heavily assumption-dependent.

The Architecture

Enterprise Value = PV of Explicit FCFs + PV of Terminal Value Where: PV of FCFt = FCFt / (1 + WACC)^t Terminal Value (Gordon Growth) = FCFn × (1+g) / (WACC - g) Terminal Value (Exit Multiple) = EBITDAn × Exit EV/EBITDA Multiple Then: Equity Value = EV - Net Debt - Minority Interest - Preferred Implied Share Price = Equity Value / Diluted Shares

Step 1 — Project Free Cash Flow

FCF (unlevered) = EBIT × (1 − tax rate) + D&A − Capex − ΔNWC

Start with revenue growth assumptions — driven by historical CAGR, management guidance, and industry data. Then build operating margins (EBITDA, EBIT). Apply a normalised tax rate. Add back non-cash D&A. Subtract maintenance and growth capex. Adjust for working capital movements.

The explicit forecast period is typically 5 years for mature businesses, 10 years for high-growth businesses. Beyond the forecast period, you need a terminal value assumption.

FCF DriverWhere it comes fromKey assumption risk
Revenue growthHistorical CAGR, management guidance, market dataOveroptimism; cyclicality not reflected
EBITDA marginHistorical margins + operating leverage + synergiesMargin expansion often assumed without operational evidence
CapexManagement guidance, % of revenue for sectorUnderstating maintenance capex inflates FCF
Working capitalDays ratios (DSO, DIO, DPO) from historical balance sheetsIgnoring WC build in fast-growing businesses
Tax rateEffective rate from financials; normalise for deferred taxesUsing statutory rate instead of effective rate

Step 2 — Calculate Terminal Value

Terminal value captures all cash flows beyond the explicit period. Two methods:

Gordon Growth Model: TV = FCFn × (1+g) / (WACC − g). Best for stable, mature businesses. g is typically set at long-run GDP growth (1.5–3.0% in developed markets). Only mathematically valid when WACC > g.

Exit Multiple: TV = EBITDAn × Exit EV/EBITDA. More practical — grounded in observable transaction multiples. The exit multiple should be consistent with where you expect the industry to trade in 5–10 years.

Step 3 — Discount and sum

Apply discount factors 1/(1+WACC)^t to each FCF and the terminal value. Sum to get EV. Then subtract net debt to get equity value. Divide by diluted shares for implied price.

Terminal Value Dominance

Terminal value typically represents 60–80% of total EV in a DCF. This means most of the "value" you are discounting is an assumption about what happens after Year 5 or Year 10 — a period where forecasting accuracy is very low. This is why sensitivity analysis is mandatory and why DCF ranges are always wider than comps ranges.

The Most Dangerous DCF Mistake

Setting the terminal growth rate above WACC. If g ≥ WACC, the Gordon Growth denominator becomes zero or negative — the model breaks and produces an infinitely large or nonsensical value. Always check: g must be meaningfully below WACC (at least 2–3% spread). If your "base case" requires g above WACC to produce a reasonable value, your other assumptions are wrong.

In a Real Bank

DCF models in IB are typically 200–400 row Excel files. The FCF build usually has its own dedicated tab, fed by revenue and margin assumption tabs. The WACC calculation has its own tab with peer beta collection. The output tab shows the valuation summary and sensitivity tables. Building a clean, auditable DCF model that an MD can interrogate and a client can understand is a core first-year analyst skill — and takes months to do well.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We run our own DCF on every credit we consider — not to value the equity, but to stress-test FCF against debt service. We project FCF under three scenarios (base, downside -20%, severe downside -35%) and check whether the business can service interest and amortisation in each scenario. If FCF turns negative in the downside before we are repaid, we are taking credit risk we need to price into the margin.

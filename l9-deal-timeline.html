📌 Level 09 · Deal Execution · L9.06 — Deal Timeline & Process Management

Every IB transaction runs on a timeline. Managing that timeline — keeping all parties moving at the right pace, anticipating delays, and sequencing decisions correctly — is a core execution skill. The analyst is the de facto project manager of the operational elements of every deal.

M&A Process — full milestone map

SELL-SIDE M&A PROCESS (standard mid-market, no regulatory issues): PREPARATION (weeks 1–6): Week 1-2: Kick-off; financial model; buyer long-list; CIM draft Week 3-4: CIM review with management; NDA and process letter draft Week 5-6: CIM approved; VDR populated; materials finalised FIRST ROUND (weeks 7–12): Week 7: Process letter + CIM distributed under NDA Week 8-11: Q&A management; buyer preparation Week 12: IOI deadline; shortlist analysis and recommendation SECOND ROUND (weeks 13–20): Week 13: Second-round process letter; enhanced VDR access Week 14-16: Management presentations (3-4 per week) Week 17-19: Full DD; Q&A intensive; issues list management Week 20: Binding bid deadline; bid analysis EXCLUSIVITY / NEGOTIATION (weeks 21–26): Week 21: Preferred bidder selection; exclusivity letter signed Week 22-24: SPA negotiation (legal) Week 25-26: Board approvals; SIGNING COMPLETION (weeks 27–38+): Week 27-30: Regulatory filings (if required) Week 30-38: Regulatory clearance / shareholder vote Week 38+: COMPLETION

The Analyst as Project Manager

No one formally assigns project management to the analyst — but the analyst is the only person tracking every open item, every deadline, and every dependency across the process. Senior bankers manage relationships; the analyst ensures the machinery moves.

Anticipating Delays — the Most Valuable Project Management Skill

A process runs on time when problems are anticipated rather than reacted to. The analyst who sees that management accounts have not been uploaded to the VDR and proactively chases the CFO five days before the DD phase opens saves a week of delay. The analyst who notices that the preferred bidder's SPA redline has not arrived three days before the planned signing date and escalates to the MD prevents a last-minute crisis. Reactive project management — waiting for deadlines to be missed before acting — is the junior banker's most common process failure mode.

In a Real Bank

Every major IB deal has a deal team call or email each morning during the active execution phase. The analyst typically runs the agenda: here is what was completed yesterday, here is what needs to happen today, here is what is at risk. This daily operational discipline — not the valuation models — is what keeps complex transactions on schedule. The analyst who can maintain this rhythm across multiple simultaneous deals, without dropping anything, earns a reputation that opens doors to better deals and faster promotion.

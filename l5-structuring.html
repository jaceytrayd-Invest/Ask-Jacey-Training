📌 Level 05 · M&A · L5.05 — Deal Structuring

When price cannot be agreed, structure is what closes deals. The mechanics of how a transaction is legally constructed — share vs. asset purchase, cash vs. stock, locked-box vs. completion accounts — have significant tax, accounting, and economic consequences for both parties.

Share Purchase vs. Asset Purchase

Share PurchaseAsset Purchase
What transfers100% of the legal entity — all assets AND liabilities, including hidden onesSpecific assets and specified liabilities only — buyer picks and chooses
Seller preferencePreferred — cleaner exit; capital gains tax treatment on the share disposalNot preferred — ordinary income tax on asset gains; seller retains unwanted liabilities
Buyer preferenceLess preferred — inherits all liabilities (known and unknown); no tax basis step-upPreferred — controls what is acquired; gets tax basis step-up on assets; avoids unknown liabilities
Tax basis step-upNo — buyer inherits seller's historical basisYes — assets recorded at fair market value (purchase price); higher future depreciation deductions
Third-party consentsFewer — contracts stay in the company; change of control clauses may applyMore — contracts must be novated; customers/suppliers may need to consent
Typical useMost M&A — both public and private company acquisitionsSpecific asset acquisitions; distressed deals; carve-outs from larger groups

Locked-Box vs. Completion Accounts

LOCKED-BOX: Price fixed at a historical "locked-box date" balance sheet Seller retains economic risk/reward from locked-box date → signing Seller provides "leakage" indemnity: no cash extracted between locked-box date and closing (dividends, related-party payments) Clean and simple → preferred in European / PE transactions COMPLETION ACCOUNTS: Price calculated on the ACTUAL balance sheet at closing date Complex post-close adjustment mechanism Buyer argues for lower closing adjustments; seller argues for higher Most common in US transactions; frequent source of post-close disputes Provides the buyer with precision but at the cost of certainty

Earn-Outs — bridging the valuation gap

When buyer and seller disagree on value — often because the seller believes in higher future growth than the buyer will pay for today — an earn-out defers part of the price contingent on performance milestones. Typical earn-out metric: revenue, EBITDA, or gross profit. Typical earn-out term: 1–3 years post-close.

Earn-Out Best Practices

Earn-outs are the most litigated element of M&A. Best practices to reduce conflict: define the metric precisely (exactly which revenues, which GAAP adjustments apply); specify accounting policies; give the seller information rights (quarterly management accounts); include dispute resolution (independent accountant as arbitrator); avoid using EBITDA as the metric when the acquirer controls cost allocation — a buyer can inflate costs and prevent the earn-out from paying.

In a Real Bank

Structuring is where IB, legal, and tax advice converge — and it is where experienced deal teams earn their fees most clearly. A creative structure can close a deal that would otherwise fail on price alone. Examples: adding a deferred payment tranche, offering rollover equity to founder-sellers to align post-close incentives, structuring the consideration as an asset purchase (at the seller's cost) in exchange for a meaningfully higher headline price to compensate. Understanding all available levers — not just price — is what separates senior bankers from juniors.

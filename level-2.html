Featured Lesson — Three-Statement Linkage

📍 Accounting · Three-Statement Linkage · L2.04

The most-tested accounting question in IB interviews is: "Walk me through the three financial statements and how they connect." Most candidates know what each statement is. Very few can explain the precise mechanical links. Here they are.

The Three Statements — at a glance

StatementQuestion it answersTime dimensionKey output
Income StatementHow profitable was the business?Over a period (quarter/year)Net Income
Balance SheetWhat does the business own and owe?At a point in time (snapshot)Equity (Assets − Liabilities)
Cash Flow StatementHow much cash did the business generate?Over a periodNet change in cash

The exact links between them

Link 1 — Net Income flows into the Cash Flow Statement

The CFS begins with Net Income from the P&L. From there, non-cash items (D&A) are added back, and working capital changes are adjusted.

Cash Flow from Operations starts with:
Net Income (from P&L)
+ Depreciation & Amortisation (non-cash — added back)
± Changes in Working Capital (AR, AP, Inventory)
= Cash from Operations

Link 2 — Net Income flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet

At period end, net income (less dividends) is added to Retained Earnings in Shareholders' Equity. This is how the P&L accumulates in equity over time.

Retained Earnings (end) = Retained Earnings (start) + Net Income − Dividends Paid

Link 3 — Net change in cash ties the CFS to the Balance Sheet

The bottom line of the CFS is the net increase or decrease in cash — this exactly equals the change in the Cash line on the Balance Sheet between opening and closing periods.

Cash (BS, end) = Cash (BS, start) + Net Change in Cash (CFS)

Link 4 — Capex flows from CFS to PP&E on the Balance Sheet

Capital expenditure appears as a cash outflow in CFI and simultaneously increases PP&E on the Balance Sheet. Depreciation then reduces PP&E and flows back through the P&L over time.

Link 5 — Debt changes connect CFS financing to the Balance Sheet

Debt raised or repaid appears in Cash Flow from Financing and simultaneously changes the debt balance on the Balance Sheet. Interest paid flows through the P&L as an expense, reducing net income.

Why this matters in IB

When you build a 3-statement model in Excel, these linkages are the formulas. If your balance sheet doesn't balance (Assets ≠ Liabilities + Equity), one of these five links is broken. The balance sheet balancing is your error-check on everything else.

Most Common Interview Mistake

Candidates say "net income goes to retained earnings" — correct — but forget it also feeds the CFS as the starting line. Both flows happen simultaneously. Missing the CFS link is the tell that separates genuine understanding from memorisation.

In a Real Bank

When an MD asks you to "quickly pull the financials," you open all three statements simultaneously. First check: does the cash on the balance sheet match what the CFS implies? Does the retained earnings roll forward correctly? A model that doesn't pass these checks doesn't go to the client.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

From a credit perspective, we focus on the Cash Flow Statement above all — specifically free cash flow. P&L profit can be managed through accruals; cash cannot be faked as easily. Our covenants are built around cash flow metrics — interest cover, FCF / debt service — not accounting profit. Strong net income with negative FCF is a red flag we investigate before any lending decision.

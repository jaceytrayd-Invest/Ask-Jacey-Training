📌 Level 03 · Valuation · L3.03 — Precedent Transactions

Precedent transactions tell you what acquirers have actually paid. Unlike trading comps (which reflect minority market prices), precedent multiples include the control premium — making them consistently higher and directly relevant to M&A pricing.

Why Precedents Are Always Higher Than Comps

The control premium is the additional amount above the market price that acquirers pay to gain control of a business. It typically ranges from 20–40% above the unaffected share price. This premium reflects:

  • Synergies only the acquirer can realise (cost savings, revenue uplift)
  • The right to set strategy and allocate capital without minority shareholders
  • Exit optionality — ability to sell the asset on the acquirer's own timeline
  • Competitive auction dynamics — multiple bidders drive prices up
Unaffected Share Price: £10.00 (before any rumours of a deal) Offer Price: £13.50 Control Premium: 35% = (£13.50 - £10.00) / £10.00

Key Data Sources

CapIQ and Bloomberg both have M&A transaction databases. Mergermarket is the most comprehensive for deal news and rationale. Factset also has strong M&A data. For each deal you need: announcement date, target, acquirer, deal value, deal structure (cash/stock/mix), premium paid, and LTM financial metrics of the target at time of announcement.

Strategic vs. Financial Buyer Premium

Strategic buyers (corporates) typically pay 15–25% more than financial buyers (PE) for the same asset because they can realise revenue and cost synergies that PE cannot. When your precedent set includes both strategic and PE transactions, present them separately. Applying a strategic precedent multiple to a PE deal will overstate the value.

The "Unaffected" Price — Critical Concept

The control premium should always be calculated against the unaffected share price — the price before any market rumours or leaks about a potential deal. If you use the price one day before announcement, you may be measuring against a share price already inflated by rumour. Proper precedent analysis uses the share price 1–4 weeks before any market speculation began.

Common Mistake: Using Stale Precedents

A transaction from 2017 in a sector that has fundamentally changed (e.g., media, retail, energy) may be worse than no data point at all — it can anchor the valuation to an irrelevant period. Always assess whether the market conditions and deal rationale of each precedent are comparable to the current deal before including it.

In a Real Bank

In a sell-side M&A pitch, the MD will have you add the bank's own relevant completed deals to the precedent table — this demonstrates "credentials" to the client. Every tombstone in the bank's tombstone book is a potential precedent. Deals your bank has worked on are often better understood and more defensible than third-party transactions sourced from CapIQ alone.

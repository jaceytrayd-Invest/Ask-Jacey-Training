📌 Level 06 · Capital Markets · L6.01 — ECM — Equity Capital Markets Overview

Equity Capital Markets is the business of helping companies raise equity from institutional investors. The ECM product suite spans IPOs, follow-on offerings, rights issues, convertibles, and block trades — each serving a different corporate need at a different point in a company's lifecycle.

ECM Fees — how banks earn

Gross underwriting fee: typically 3.5-7% of gross proceeds → IPO in UK: 3.5-5% for large-cap; 5-7% for smaller → US IPO: 7% is the historic standard for mid-market; less for mega deals → Follow-on (ABB): 2-4% (faster process, less work) → Block trade: 1-3% (overnight, bank takes price risk) → Convertible: 2-3.5% Fee split within syndicate: Management fee (pro-rata to role): ~20% of gross fee Underwriting fee (pro-rata to underwriting commitment): ~20% Selling concession (pro-rata to actual shares sold): ~60%
League Tables — Why Bookrunner Status Matters

Investment banking rankings ("league tables") track ECM deal volumes and fee revenue by bank. Only bookrunner and global coordinator designations receive credit in most league table methodologies — co-managers typically do not. This creates fierce competition for bookrunner status: being a co-manager on a £2bn IPO contributes almost nothing to a bank's rankings. Being a bookrunner on a £200m deal contributes meaningfully. League table position affects a bank's ability to win future mandates — clients rely on rankings as a credibility signal.

In a Real Bank

ECM analysts work across three distinct workstreams simultaneously. Live deal execution (prospectus, model, investor materials, order book). New business origination support (pitch books for potential new mandates). Market monitoring (tracking equity market conditions, comparable deals, sector performance). The "market window" concept is critical in ECM — conditions change rapidly, and the difference between a successful IPO and a pulled deal can be a single week of market volatility. ECM bankers must understand macro as fluently as they understand corporate finance.

📌 Level 05 · M&A · L5.02 — The M&A Process — Sell-Side

The sell-side M&A process is a choreographed auction — designed to create competition among buyers and maximise price for the seller. Understanding each stage, and the documents and decisions involved, is the operational knowledge that separates analysts who understand deals from those who just model them.

The Six Stages of a Sell-Side Process

StageDurationKey deliverablesDecision made
1. Mandate & Preparation2–4 weeksEngagement letter; initial financial model; long list of potential buyers; NDA templatesWhich buyers to approach; process structure (broad vs. targeted)
2. Marketing Phase4–8 weeksCIM drafted and approved; management presentation prepared; data room populated; process letter sentWhich documents disclose to which buyers; data room permissions
3. First Round — Indicative Bids2–4 weeksIOIs (Indications of Interest) received; shortlist selected for second round (typically 3–5 parties)Who proceeds to second round based on price, conditionality, and credibility
4. Due Diligence4–8 weeksFull VDR access; management Q&A sessions; legal, financial, tax, commercial DD; expert sessionsOngoing — which buyer queries are answered; which areas of sensitivity to manage
5. Final Bids & Negotiation2–4 weeksBinding offers received; SPA markups exchanged; exclusivity granted to preferred bidderPreferred bidder selection (price, conditionality, speed, certainty); exclusivity length
6. Sign & Close4–16 weeksSPA finalised and signed; regulatory filings (antitrust, FDI); shareholder votes; completionRegulatory approval strategy; shareholder communication; closing mechanics

Why Competition Matters

The fundamental goal of a sell-side auction is to create a credible competitive threat. Even if only one buyer is genuinely interested, the seller's bank will structure the process to make every buyer believe others are equally motivated. The threat of losing the deal to a competitor is what drives buyers to submit their best price. A well-run auction consistently achieves a 10–20% higher price than a bilateral negotiation.

The Analyst's Most Common Process Mistakes

VDR permissions errors. Sharing a document with the wrong buyer — or accidentally revealing a buyer's identity to a competitor — can contaminate the process and expose the bank to liability. Every document upload must be checked for permissions.

Q&A log management. All buyer questions must be answered consistently across parties — the same answer to the same question from every buyer. Inconsistent answers create legal risk. The sell-side bank manages a master Q&A log throughout the DD phase.

In a Real Bank

During the due diligence phase, junior analysts on the sell-side spend the majority of their time on two tasks: populating and organising the data room (hundreds of documents, carefully indexed and permissioned), and managing the Q&A log (tracking questions from multiple buyers, coordinating with management to draft answers, and distributing responses). It is detailed, unglamorous work — but errors here can kill a deal or generate legal liability. Precision and process discipline are the skills that matter most in this phase.

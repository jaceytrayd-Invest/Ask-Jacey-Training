📌 Level 11 · Advanced · L11.03 — Advanced Modelling Techniques

Advanced modelling is not about complexity for its own sake — it is about choosing the right tool for each analytical question. Sum-of-the-parts, full merger models, Monte Carlo simulation, and real options represent the techniques that senior analysts use when standard approaches are insufficient.

Sum-of-the-Parts — the conglomerate lens

When to use: multi-division company where a blended multiple misrepresents value Build steps: 1. Identify segments from company reporting 2. Select the right methodology per segment: High-growth SaaS: EV/NTM Revenue (e.g., 7.5x) Mature industrial: EV/EBITDA (e.g., 9.0x) Financial services: Price/Book Property/infrastructure: NAV or yield-based 3. Value each segment independently with its own peer set 4. Capitalise corporate overhead: Annual overhead / EBITDA multiple 5. Sum: Σ(segment EVs) − Capitalised overhead = SOTP EV 6. Bridge: SOTP EV − Net Debt = SOTP Equity Value 7. Compare to market value: gap = conglomerate discount or premium Conglomerate discount: SOTP > Market Value → Implies value unlock opportunity from separation (spin-off catalyst) → Typical discount: 10-20% for large diversified conglomerates

The Full Merger Model

A merger model combines two companies' financials and computes pro forma EPS. More complex than the simplified A/D analysis — it models the full combined P&L. Structure: Tab 1: Acquirer standalone (3-year forecast) Tab 2: Target standalone (3-year forecast) Tab 3: Deal mechanics (price, consideration split, financing) Tab 4: PPA (purchase price allocation — intangibles, goodwill) Tab 5: Synergies (phased cost and revenue synergies) Tab 6: Combined P&L (merging all above) Tab 7: A/D output (pro forma EPS vs standalone) Key mechanics unique to the merger model: PPA amortisation: intangibles created at acquisition amortise, reducing NI Interest on acquisition debt: after-tax cost on cash consideration New shares issued: for stock consideration — dilutes share count Synergy timing: phase-in over 2-3 years with integration costs in Year 1

Monte Carlo Simulation — when ranges matter

A DCF produces a point estimate. But inputs like revenue growth rate and WACC are not points — they are distributions. Monte Carlo simulation runs the model thousands of times with random draws from each input distribution, producing a probability distribution of outcomes rather than a single value.

Application: DCF with uncertain inputs Inputs defined as distributions (not point estimates): Revenue growth Y1-Y5: Normal distribution, mean 8%, std dev 3% EBITDA margin: Normal distribution, mean 22%, std dev 2.5% WACC: Uniform distribution between 9% and 12% Terminal growth: Normal distribution, mean 2.5%, std dev 0.5% Monte Carlo output (after 10,000 simulations): Mean EV: £485m Median EV: £471m 5th pct: £312m (downside scenario probability) 95th pct: £698m (upside scenario probability) P(EV > £400m): 72% This output answers a different — and more honest — question: "What is the probability that this business is worth more than the proposed offer price of £450m?" Answer: 63%.
Real Options Valuation

Standard DCF assumes management will execute the business plan as projected. In reality, management has options: to expand (invest more if the business succeeds), to delay (wait for better conditions), to abandon (exit if the business fails). These options have real value — but DCF ignores them. Real options analysis applies option pricing theory (Black-Scholes or binomial) to value managerial flexibility. It is most valuable in: (1) natural resource extraction (the option to mine or not mine based on commodity prices); (2) pharma R&D (the option to proceed to Phase II only if Phase I succeeds); (3) staged infrastructure investment. In IB, understanding that a company with embedded real options is worth more than its DCF suggests is conceptually important even when formal real options pricing is not used.

In a Real Bank

The most valuable modelling skill at the senior analyst / associate level is not knowing exotic techniques — it is building models that are flexible, auditable, and stable under stress testing. A merger model that crashes when the deal consideration changes from cash to stock is worse than useless. A Monte Carlo simulation that a client cannot understand or interrogate adds no value. Advanced techniques earn their place when they provide genuinely better insight than simpler approaches — not when they signal technical sophistication to the analyst's own satisfaction.

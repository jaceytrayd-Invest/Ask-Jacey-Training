📌 Level 10 · Careers · L10.01 — Recruitment — Getting the Interview

The IB application process is competitive by design. Hundreds of applications compete for a handful of analyst spots at any given bank. The candidates who reach interview stage share one characteristic: they made it easy for a screener to say yes in 30 seconds. Understanding how screening works — and how to present yourself against those criteria — is the first strategic step.

What the CV Screen Actually Assesses

A recruiter spends approximately 30 seconds on each CV. In those 30 seconds, they are asking three questions:

1. ACADEMIC CREDENTIALS: Is the degree from a target university? Is the grade in the top bracket? Target universities vary by bank but generally include: LSE, Oxford, Cambridge, Imperial, UCL, Warwick, Durham (UK); Ivy League, LSE/Oxbridge exchange (US) Grade: 2:1 minimum; strong preference for 1st (UK) or 3.7+ GPA (US) Note: Exceptional candidates from non-target universities do break in — but they need stronger finance experience and networking to compensate. 2. RELEVANT EXPERIENCE: Is there evidence of finance interest and capability? High-signal: IB internship (any bank), PE/VC internship, M&A advisory work, quantitative role, financial services with analytical content Medium-signal: accounting firm, consulting, corporate treasury Low-signal: retail banking branch, non-analytical financial services 3. DEMONSTRATED INTEREST: Has this person done anything that signals genuine engagement with finance beyond coursework? CFA Level 1 or passed, financial modelling courses, investment club, equity research (student fund, personal portfolio analysis), specific deals or companies tracked with analytical depth
Networking — the Most Underused Tool

A referral from an analyst or associate at a bank can move an application from the pile to the screened-first category. This is not about calling in favours — it is about establishing genuine connections through informational conversations. The process: identify 5–10 analysts at your target banks who share your background (same university, same degree programme, same industry interest). Send a brief, specific email: "I am preparing applications for IB analyst roles. I would welcome 15 minutes to understand your experience in [group/sector] — I have specific questions about [X]." Most analysts respond to targeted, respectful outreach. The conversation leads to a relationship. The relationship leads to a referral or at minimum to inside knowledge about what the bank values.

In a Real Bank

The summer internship is the single most important application you will make. Most major banks fill 70–90% of their analyst class from converted interns. A strong internship offer is effectively a 10-week interview — and it comes before the full-time application season, which means converting it removes the need to compete in the most crowded application market. Prioritise internship applications above all else in the penultimate year.

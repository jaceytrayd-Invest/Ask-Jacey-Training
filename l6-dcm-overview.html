📌 Level 06 · Capital Markets · L6.03 — DCM — Debt Capital Markets Overview

Debt Capital Markets (DCM) is the business of helping companies, governments, and financial institutions raise debt through publicly distributed instruments — primarily bonds and syndicated loans. DCM is larger by volume than ECM and operates at a faster pace, with investment grade bonds often pricing in a single day.

IG Bond Issuance — how it works

An investment grade bond issuance (for a large, rated corporate) can be completed in a single day. The bank receives the mandate, begins sounding out investor appetite at 8am, launches the deal with initial price thoughts at 10am, builds an order book through the morning, tightens the spread by midday, and prices the bond by 2pm. Settlement occurs 5 business days later (T+5).

Same-day IG bond execution timeline: 08:00 — Mandate received; bank begins investor sounding calls 08:30 — Initial Price Thoughts (IPT) announced: "around Gilts + 150bps area" 09:00 — Formal launch; investor orders begin flowing to bookrunner 10:30 — Update: book covered 2x at IPT level; spread guidance tightened 11:30 — Final Terms: Gilts + 135bps; book covered 4x; price set 12:00 — Books close; allocation begins 14:00 — Deal announced to market; bonds begin trading on a grey market T+5 — Settlement; company receives proceeds

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

Private credit (direct lending) is increasingly displacing the syndicated loan market for mid-market companies. Instead of a bank syndicating a loan to 20+ lenders, a single private credit fund (like JaceyTrayd's strategy) provides the full facility directly. Benefits for the borrower: faster execution, certainty of close, flexible structuring, covenant flexibility. Benefit for the lender: higher margin, stronger covenant package, direct relationship. The trade-off: private credit is less liquid than a traded syndicated loan — we cannot sell our position easily if the credit deteriorates. We price this illiquidity premium into our lending margin.

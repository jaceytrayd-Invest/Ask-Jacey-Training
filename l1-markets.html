📌 Level 01 — Foundations · L1.01 — Financial Markets — Structure & Participants

Financial markets are the infrastructure through which capital moves from those who have it to those who need it. Understanding the structure of these markets — who participates, how they interact, and how pricing works — is the foundation of financial literacy.

Primary vs. Secondary Markets

Primary markets are where new securities are issued for the first time. When a company does an IPO or issues new bonds, it is raising capital in the primary market. The company receives the proceeds. Investment banks facilitate primary market transactions as underwriters and advisors.

Secondary markets are where already-issued securities trade between investors. The London Stock Exchange, New York Stock Exchange, and bond trading platforms are secondary markets. The company does not receive proceeds from secondary trading — investors buy and sell from each other. Secondary markets provide liquidity to investors who bought in the primary market.

How Markets Connect to IB

Investment banking is fundamentally a market-access business. Banks help clients access primary markets (IPOs, bond issuances) and provide advice on transactions (M&A) that ultimately result in secondary market trading. When interest rates rise (driven by central banks), the cost of debt increases, DCF discount rates rise, and M&A volumes typically slow — because deals are harder to finance at the same returns. Market conditions permeate every aspect of IB.

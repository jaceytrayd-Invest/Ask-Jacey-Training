📌 Level 07 · Derivatives · L7.06 — Structured Products & Convertibles

Structured products combine conventional instruments (bonds, loans, equities) with derivatives to create custom risk-return profiles unavailable from either component alone. Convertible bonds are the most common structured product in IB — but warrants, collars, CVRs, and CPPI structures also appear regularly in deal contexts.

Structured Products — the building block concept

Any structured product can be decomposed into conventional instruments plus derivatives. The key questions are always: what does the investor own? What have they given up? And what risk-return trade-off has been created?

Warrants — how they differ from options

A warrant is similar to a call option — but with two key differences. First, warrants are issued by the company itself (not by a third party), so when exercised, the company issues new shares — causing dilution. Second, warrants have longer tenors (3–10 years) versus exchange-listed options (typically under 2 years).

Warrants appear in IB in two main contexts: attached to high yield bonds or mezzanine debt to sweeten the yield-to-risk trade-off for lenders (the equity kicker), and in SPAC structures where sponsor warrants are a core component of the SPAC economics.

Contingent Value Rights (CVRs) in M&A

A CVR is an M&A bridge instrument — it pays the target's shareholders an additional amount if a specified post-close event occurs. Common in pharmaceutical M&A: an acquirer buys a biotech and issues CVRs that pay $X per share if the lead drug receives FDA approval within 3 years. This bridges the valuation gap between a buyer who discounts the drug's approval probability and a seller who is more optimistic. Economically, a CVR is a binary option on the approval event. CVRs can be listed and tradeable, giving sellers liquidity on their contingent claim.

In a Real Bank

Convertible bond analysis sits at the intersection of ECM (equity characteristics) and DCM (bond characteristics) and the derivatives desk (option valuation). The IB analyst typically works with: the equity capital markets team (for the equity story and conversion premium target), the debt capital markets team (for the bond floor calculation and credit assessment), and the equity derivatives desk (for the Black-Scholes option valuation). Getting all three teams aligned on the terms of a new convertible is one of the more complex multi-team coordination challenges in IB deal execution.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We sometimes receive warrants as part of our private credit deal economics — particularly in higher-risk or growth-stage situations where the interest rate alone does not adequately compensate for the risk. A warrant giving us the right to buy 5% of the company's equity at today's valuation provides additional upside if the company succeeds. We value warrants separately from the debt in our portfolio reporting — they are equity instruments and carry equity risk. We use Black-Scholes for valuation at each reporting period, with volatility derived from either listed comparable companies or implied from secondary transaction activity in the borrower's sector.

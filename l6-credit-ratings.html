📌 Level 06 · Capital Markets · L6.05 — Credit Ratings & the Rating Process

Credit ratings are the gatekeeping mechanism of debt capital markets. They determine which investor base a company can access, at what cost, and under what terms. Understanding how ratings are assigned — and what moves them — is essential for any capital markets professional.

How Ratings Are Assigned

Rating agencies use a combination of quantitative and qualitative factors. The process for a new rating:

StepWhat happensTimeline
1. MandateCompany engages the rating agency and pays the rating feeDay 1
2. Information packageCompany submits financial statements, business plan, management presentationsDays 1–5
3. Analyst reviewAgency analysts review materials, build financial models, assess peer comparisonsWeeks 1–3
4. Management meetingFace-to-face or virtual Q&A — agency asks about strategy, risk, financial policyWeek 2–3
5. Rating committeeAnalysts present recommendation; committee votes; rating determinedWeek 3–4
6. Notification and appealCompany notified of rating; may appeal factual errors (not the opinion)Week 4
7. PublicationRating published publicly with full rationaleDay after notification

The "Fallen Angel" — why IG-to-HY downgrades are so disruptive

Before downgrade (BBB-): Eligible for: IG bond indices, pension fund mandates, insurance portfolios Investor base: global, broad, price-insensitive (hold to maturity) Borrowing cost: Gilts + 150-200bps After downgrade (BB+): Forced selling by: IG index trackers (must replicate IG index weights) IG-mandate pension/insurance funds (regulatory constraint) Estimated forced selling volume: 20-40% of outstanding bonds Immediate spread impact: +100 to +200bps widening on downgrade day New borrowing cost: Gilts + 350-450bps (or 300bps wider than pre-downgrade) Impact on the company: Existing bonds: price falls sharply on announcement New debt: much more expensive if issued Bank facilities: some IG-linked covenants may trigger M&A capacity: debt-funded acquisitions become more expensive Management focus: enormous distraction; investor relations consumed
Rating Outlook vs. Rating Watch

Outlook (Stable/Positive/Negative/Developing): The agency's view of where the rating is headed over 12–24 months. A Negative Outlook does not mean a downgrade is imminent — it means conditions may warrant one. Issuers typically have time to respond.

Rating Watch Negative/Positive: More urgent — a specific event (pending acquisition, regulatory decision, quarterly results) may trigger a rating change within 3 months. Rating Watch Negative is often placed at M&A announcement — the agency is reviewing whether the combined entity retains IG status post-deal.

In a Real Bank

In a DCM advisory context, the bank models the "rating impact" of every proposed transaction before presenting it to the client. "If you execute this £500m acquisition using debt, we estimate your leverage rises to 3.8x — which puts you at risk of a Negative Outlook from S&P and potentially a one-notch downgrade. The cost of that downgrade (wider spreads, forced selling) may exceed the synergy value of the acquisition." This rating headroom analysis is presented alongside the accretion/dilution model in every large corporate finance advisory.

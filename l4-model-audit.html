📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.07 — Model Audit & Error-Checking

Every financial model contains errors — the difference between good and bad analysts is whether those errors are found before the model reaches a client. Model auditing is a systematic discipline, not a casual review. Apply it every time, without exception.

The Model Audit Checklist

Run these checks in order every time you complete or update a model before sending:

Common Error Patterns

Off-by-one-period errors. The most common modelling error. Year 3 balance sheet references Year 3 cash flow instead of Year 2 (prior period). The formula is correct conceptually but references the wrong column. Always check that opening balance = prior year closing balance — use Ctrl+[ to trace precedents.

SUM range doesn't extend to new rows. You add a new debt tranche or a new P&L line — but the SUM formula above it doesn't include the new row. The model looks correct but is missing a line. Audit all SUM formulas to confirm their ranges are comprehensive.

Linking to an old version of the model. A formula in the Output tab links to a cell in the Assumptions tab that was moved when the model was restructured. The formula now points to an empty or wrong cell. All cross-tab references should be checked when the model structure changes.

Ctrl+[ and Ctrl+] — Your Debugging Tools

Ctrl+[ (trace precedents): shows which cells feed into the selected cell. Use this to verify that a formula is pointing to the right source.

Ctrl+] (trace dependents): shows which cells use the selected cell as an input. Use this to verify that a change in an assumption cascades to all the right places — and nowhere unexpected.

These two shortcuts, combined with Ctrl+~ (toggle formula display), are the three most important Excel keyboard shortcuts for model auditing.

The Error That Reaches Clients

The model error that most often survives to reach a client is one where the model balance check passes (balance sheet = 0) but a formula elsewhere produces a wrong number that happens to be internally consistent. Example: the debt schedule correctly reduces debt by £10m due to a sweep — but the sweep formula is wrong and should have been £15m. The balance sheet still balances (cash is £5m lower than it should be, debt is £5m higher), but the equity value is wrong. The only protection: also check that specific outputs (EV, equity value, debt level) make intuitive sense in the context of the deal.

In a Real Bank

Before any model goes to an MD or to a client, many banks require a "model sign-off" from a senior associate or junior vice president who did not build the model — a fresh pair of eyes. This person runs through the standard audit checklist and flags any issues. The builder fixes them. This separation of duties is a simple control that catches the vast majority of errors before they reach the market. In your career, be the analyst who proactively asks for a model review — not the one who gets called at midnight because a client spotted an error.

📌 Level 01 — Foundations · L1.06 — The Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

Private credit has become one of the most important forces in deal financing. Understanding how lenders think — what they analyse, what protects them, and how they differ from equity investors — is essential for any IB practitioner working on leveraged transactions.

How Lenders Think Differently from Equity Investors

Equity InvestorLender (JaceyTrayd)
GoalMaximise upside; unlimited return potentialPreserve capital; earn contractual return; avoid loss
FocusGrowth, market opportunity, management visionCash flow stability, asset coverage, downside scenarios
Key metricsRevenue growth, EBITDA margin expansion, TAM, P/ENet Leverage, Interest Cover, FCF/Debt Service, DSO, LTV
Scenario planningBase case and upside focusBase case and downside — "what if EBITDA falls 30%?"
Time horizonOpen-ended; sell when value is realisedFixed — loan matures at a specific date; want repayment

What JaceyTrayd Analyses Before Lending

1. Cash flow quality. Can the borrower service interest from operations in a downside scenario? We build a stressed FCF model — base, −20% EBITDA, −35% EBITDA. Interest cover must exceed 2.0× even in the −20% scenario.

2. Collateral quality. If the borrower defaults, what assets can we enforce against? Receivables, inventory, property, IP, brand value. We assess each asset's realisable value in a distressed sale (not book value).

3. Capital structure position. Where do we sit in the waterfall? Are there senior-ranking creditors above us? How large is the equity cushion below us? Our loan-to-value ratio must leave sufficient equity buffer.

4. Management team and governance. Do we trust management to operate the business responsibly? Is there a controlling shareholder or PE sponsor with skin in the game? Strong sponsorship is a significant credit positive.

Covenants — the lender's early warning system

Typical financial covenants in a private credit deal: 1. Net Leverage covenant: Net Debt / EBITDA ≤ 5.5x (stepping to 4.5x) 2. Interest Cover: EBITDA / Cash Interest ≥ 2.5x 3. Capex limit: Annual capex ≤ £[X]m (restricts discretionary spend) 4. Minimum liquidity: Undrawn RCF + cash ≥ £[X]m 5. Change of control: Lender consent required for ownership change Tested: Quarterly against audited or management accounts Breach consequence: Not automatic default — triggers negotiation; lender gains leverage to improve terms or require early repayment
Why IB Analysts Need to Understand Lenders

In any leveraged transaction, lenders are the primary constraint on deal structure. Their credit requirements set the maximum debt level, the minimum equity contribution, the acceptable covenant package, and often the timing of the deal. An IB analyst who does not understand lender thinking cannot structure a deal that banks will actually finance. The best M&A analysts think simultaneously from the equity perspective (is the price right?) and the lender perspective (can we finance this?).

🔑 JaceyTrayd — Our Credit Process

At JaceyTrayd, our credit approval process begins with a Borrower Assessment covering the five Cs: Character (management integrity and track record), Capacity (cash flow ability to service the debt), Capital (equity contribution and net worth), Collateral (assets available as security), and Conditions (market environment and loan structure terms). No deal proceeds without satisfactory assessment on all five dimensions.

📌 Level 03 · Valuation · L3.01 — Valuation Framework & The Football Field

Every valuation starts with a framework question: what are we measuring, and why? The football field chart is the output that brings all three methodologies together into one view. Before you touch a spreadsheet, understand the architecture.

Why Three Methodologies?

No single valuation method is definitively correct. Each methodology captures a different dimension of value and reflects different assumptions about what a business is worth:

MethodLogicAnchored toLimitation
Trading CompsWhat does the market pay for similar businesses today?Current public market pricesMarket sentiment can be irrational or distorted
Precedent TransactionsWhat have acquirers historically paid for similar businesses?Actual deal prices (including control premium)Historical deals may be stale; market conditions change
DCFWhat is this business worth based on its own intrinsic cash generation?Company-specific forecast assumptionsExtremely sensitive to WACC and growth assumptions

The overlap between all three methods is the most defensible price range. If comps say £400–500m, precedents say £480–580m, and DCF says £420–620m, the zone where all three overlap (£480–500m) is where you would anchor a recommendation.

The Football Field — anatomy

The football field is a horizontal bar chart. Each methodology gets one bar, spanning from the low to high implied equity value or EV under that approach. The bars are stacked vertically so comparisons are immediate. Additional reference points — 52-week trading range, analyst price targets, current share price — are often added as reference lines.

Reading the Football Field

Where bars overlap is the credible range. Where they diverge requires explanation. If DCF is significantly above all market-based methods, either your growth assumptions are too aggressive, or the market is significantly undervaluing the business. A pitch book without a paragraph explaining the divergence is incomplete.

Which methodology is most reliable?

The honest answer: it depends on context. In a buoyant M&A market with many recent comparable deals, precedent transactions are highly informative. In a distressed market where public valuations have collapsed, trading comps may understate intrinsic value and DCF provides a useful anchor. In a sector with no recent deals, precedent transactions are stale and you must rely more heavily on comps and DCF.

The practical answer for an analyst: present all three, acknowledge limitations of each, and use the overlap to form your recommendation. Never present only the methodology that produces the most convenient answer for your client's position.

In a Real Bank

The football field slide is typically slide 8–12 in a pitch book. It comes after market and industry analysis, and before the detailed discussion of transaction structure and process. MDs spend significant time reviewing this slide before client meetings — if the ranges are not defensible, you will be asked to redo the analysis. The football field is also the slide that gets picked apart in fairness opinions by opposing counsel — every range must be justified by documented methodology.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

From a lending perspective, we focus on the low end of the valuation range — not the midpoint or high end. If the lowest credible valuation (trough comps, conservative DCF) exceeds our loan exposure by a comfortable margin (typically 1.5-2.0x), we have adequate coverage. This is why lenders and equity investors read the same football field very differently: we care about the floor; they care about the upside.

📌 Level 09 · Deal Execution · L9.02 — Winning Mandates — The Beauty Contest

A mandate is won before the pitch book is finished. The selection decision is shaped by relationship history, sector credibility, and strategic framing — not just by the quality of the valuation analysis. Understanding what clients actually use to choose their bank is essential for any banker who wants to originate business.

The Beauty Contest — how banks compete

A beauty contest (or "bake-off") is the process by which a company selects its investment bank for a transaction. The company typically invites 3–5 banks to pitch, evaluates them, and selects one (or two) to run the mandate.

Fee Negotiation — the economics of winning

Standard M&A advisory fee structures: Retainer (monthly): £50,000–£200,000/month during execution Success fee: 0.50–1.50% of total deal value (on completion) Minimum fee: Usually £3–5m (prevents effort on deals that fail at low price) Example: £400m deal at 0.75% = £3.0m success fee + £200k retainer × 8 months = £4.6m total (Note: retainer often credited against success fee) When banks pitch BELOW market rate: Strategic client development: first deal at a discount buys the relationship League table positioning: a prestigious tombstone is worth discounting for Competitive markets: if five banks want the same mandate, pricing compresses New sector entry: bank pays for expertise-building via below-market first deal When banks can charge ABOVE market rate: Unique buyer relationships not available elsewhere Track record of higher prices achieved in recent comparable sales Specialist sector expertise genuinely unavailable at competing banks Urgent timeline where the bank has committed resources already deployed
The Real Selection Decision

Most beauty contests are decided before any bank pitches — by the relationship the coverage banker has built over years. The MD who has had quarterly dinners with the CFO, provided market updates without asking for anything, and been available for informal advice during the board's strategic deliberations already has an inside track. The pitch book confirms the choice the client has already made emotionally. This is why IB relationship coverage is a full-time job — deal origination is a long-cycle business where trust is built over years, not in a 90-minute pitch meeting.

In a Real Bank

Analysts rarely attend pitch meetings — that is the MD and Director's stage. But the analyst prepares everything the MD will say. When the MD says "we recently sold a comparable business in your sector at 14× EBITDA — here's how we structured that process," they are drawing on data the analyst assembled. When a client asks a technical question about the comp set or the DCF assumptions, the MD either knows the answer because they reviewed the model thoroughly, or they turn to the analyst who is in the room as support. The analyst's job in a pitch meeting: be a quietly reliable reference; never undermine the MD's credibility; know every number cold.

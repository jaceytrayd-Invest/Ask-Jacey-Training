📌 Level 08 · Private Markets · L8.05 — Covenants — Design & Enforcement

Covenants are the lender's early warning system — the contractual tripwires that alert the lender to deterioration before a company reaches financial distress. Understanding how covenants are designed, what triggers a breach, and how lenders actually respond when things go wrong is essential for anyone working in leveraged finance or private credit.

What Happens When a Covenant Breaches

Step 1: Identification (day 0-5) Borrower tests covenants against quarterly accounts Identifies breach (e.g., Net Leverage 5.8x vs. 5.0x covenant) Notifies lender per loan agreement (typically within 3-5 business days) Lender issues formal breach notice — begins standstill period Step 2: Standstill (day 5-30) No new drawdowns on RCF No distributions or payments to shareholders Borrower provides updated trading and cash flow forecasts Lender reviews recovery plan and management credibility Step 3: Negotiation (day 30-90) Waiver: lender waives the specific breach for a defined period Reset: covenant level adjusted (e.g., 5.0x → 6.0x for 2 quarters) Cure: PE sponsor injects equity to reduce net debt Consideration: lender receives improved economics for granting waiver → Margin step-up (typically +75-150bps) → Enhanced reporting (monthly accounts, additional KPIs) → Cash sweep requirement (50-75% of excess FCF) → Additional security → Tighter subsequent covenants Step 4: Resolution or Enforcement (if no agreement) If waiver not agreed: technical default → acceleration rights Lender can demand immediate repayment of entire outstanding balance If borrower cannot pay: enforcement of security → receivership Appointment of administrator or LPA receiver over secured assets

Equity Cure — the sponsor's rescue mechanism

How an equity cure works: Covenant breach: Net Leverage = 6.0x (covenant 5.0x) EBITDA = £20m, Net Debt = £120m (120/20 = 6.0x) To cure: PE sponsor injects £10m equity as debt repayment New Net Debt = £120m - £10m = £110m New leverage = £110m / £20m = 5.5x (still breached) Need to inject £20m to reach 5.0x: £120m - £20m = £100m / £20m = 5.0x ✓ Limitations in the loan agreement (JaceyTrayd standard): - Maximum 2 equity cures per rolling 4-quarter period - Maximum 5 equity cures over the life of the loan - Cured amount counts as "deemed EBITDA" rather than actual EBITDA (cannot be used twice in subsequent tests) - PE sponsor must confirm in writing the equity injection is not itself funded by debt at the portfolio company level
Covenant Headroom — the Lender's Safety Margin

At JaceyTrayd, we set covenants with deliberate headroom between the expected performance and the breach threshold. If we underwrite to a base case Net Leverage of 4.2x with a 5.0x covenant, the borrower has 0.8x of buffer. We then stress-test: at what EBITDA decline does this borrower breach the covenant? (Answer: EBITDA must fall approximately 19% from base case.) Is that plausible? If yes, the covenant threshold is not conservative enough — we tighten it. This headroom analysis is as important as the absolute covenant level.

🔑 JaceyTrayd — What We Actually Do When a Borrower Struggles

Our first instinct is always to work with the borrower — not to enforce. Enforcement is expensive, disruptive, and rarely maximises recovery. When a borrower misses budget and triggers a covenant concern, our response is: (1) increase contact frequency (weekly calls rather than monthly); (2) request updated forecasts within 10 days; (3) if performance is genuinely deteriorating rather than just a one-quarter blip, initiate a formal waiver discussion; (4) use the waiver negotiation to reset terms that better protect us going forward. Only when management has lost credibility, the business model is structurally impaired, or the sponsor has ceased to be constructive do we consider moving to enforcement.

📌 Level 07 · Derivatives · L7.02 — Options — Calls, Puts & Payoff Profiles

Options are the most versatile derivative. The asymmetry between buyer and seller — the buyer's limited downside, the seller's capped upside — creates unique risk management and investment possibilities unavailable with any other instrument. Master the four basic payoffs before anything else.

The Asymmetry — why options are unique

Every derivative except an option creates a symmetric obligation — both parties must transact regardless of where the market moves. An option buyer pays a premium to acquire a right: the ability to decide whether to transact based on how the market moves. This optional nature creates the defining asymmetry:

Option BuyerOption Seller
Maximum lossPremium paid — nothing moreTheoretically unlimited (call) or very large (put)
Maximum gainTheoretically unlimited (call) or capped at strike (put)Premium received — nothing more
Decision powerChooses whether to exerciseNo choice — must perform if buyer exercises
Position on volatilityLong volatility (benefits from large moves)Short volatility (benefits from small moves)

Option Value Components

Option Value = Intrinsic Value + Time Value Intrinsic Value: Call: max(S − K, 0) (value if exercised immediately) Put: max(K − S, 0) Time Value: = Option Market Price − Intrinsic Value Always positive while time remains Reflects probability of becoming more profitable before expiry Driven by: volatility, time to expiry, interest rates At expiry: Time value → zero (theta decay accelerates near expiry) Option price = Intrinsic value only

The Black-Scholes Inputs — intuition only

InputEffect on call priceEffect on put priceWhy
Underlying price (S) rises↑ Increases↓ DecreasesCall more likely ITM; put less likely ITM
Strike price (K) rises↓ Decreases↑ IncreasesCall harder to reach ITM; put easier to reach ITM
Time to expiry (T) rises↑ Increases↑ IncreasesMore time = more chance of reaching ITM
Volatility (σ) rises↑ Increases↑ IncreasesMore volatility = wider outcome range = more chance of deep ITM
Risk-free rate (r) rises↑ Increases (slightly)↓ Decreases (slightly)Higher rates reduce PV of strike; complex effect
Dividends paid↓ Decreases↑ IncreasesDividends reduce stock price on ex-date; affects call vs. put value
Options in M&A — Practical Appearances

Convertible bonds: Embedded long call on equity. Bondholder has the right to convert at the conversion price — a call on the issuer's shares.

Employee stock options (ESOs): Long calls on company equity granted at-the-money. Employees profit only if the stock rises above the grant-date price.

Break fees: In M&A, a reverse break fee (paid by the buyer if the deal falls through due to financing failure) is economically like a put option — the seller's protection against deal failure.

CVRs (Contingent Value Rights): Rights that pay out if a specific event occurs (e.g., drug approval, litigation outcome). Economically similar to binary options on a specific event.

In a Real Bank

IBD analysts rarely need to price options using Black-Scholes — that is the equity derivatives desk's job. But understanding option pricing inputs is essential for: (1) convertible bond analysis (the embedded option value drives the coupon discount); (2) IFRS 2 expense calculations for employee share schemes; (3) structuring M&A deal protection mechanisms; (4) understanding why companies issue warrants alongside debt in stressed situations. The question is never "can you derive Black-Scholes" — it is "do you understand what drives the value of the option embedded in this structure?"

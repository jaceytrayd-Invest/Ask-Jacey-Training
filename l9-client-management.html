📌 Level 09 · Deal Execution · L9.05 — Client Management & Communication

The technical work wins the mandate. Client management keeps it. How a bank communicates with a client during a live process — the cadence, the tone, the quality of updates, and the handling of bad news — determines whether the client trusts the bank with future mandates. Understanding these dynamics from day one shapes how junior bankers think about the relationships they are building.

Communicating Bad News

The most important client communication is the delivery of bad news — a bid that came in below expectations, a buyer who dropped out, a DD issue that will chip the price. The instinct is to delay or soften; the professional requirement is to communicate clearly and immediately.

The bad news communication framework: 1. Lead with the headline (do not bury it): "Three bids came in. The highest is £180m — below the £200m+ range we discussed." Not: "We've been reflecting on the bids and while there are some positive signals..." 2. Provide the context immediately: "The gap reflects the current credit market — buyers are underwriting at 4.5x leverage vs. the 5.5x we assumed in our model. This is a market-wide constraint, not a view on the business quality." 3. Present the options: "We see three paths: (a) proceed at £180m; (b) re-engage one buyer who indicated £195m was possible with additional management presentation; (c) defer the process 6 months for market conditions to improve." 4. Make a recommendation: "We recommend option (b) — the incremental time cost is low and the potential upside is £15m." The principle: clients hire bankers for judgment, not comfort. A banker who delivers bad news clearly and with a path forward is more valuable than one who soften it and leaves the client uncertain.
The Analyst's Role in Client Relationships

Analysts do not lead client relationships — but they shape them significantly. The client's finance team interacts with the analyst daily during a live process. If the analyst is responsive, accurate, professional, and proactive (flagging issues before being asked), the client's team forms a positive view of the bank. If the analyst is slow to respond, makes errors, or needs to be chased, the client notices — and mentions it to the MD. Treat every email to the client's team as client-facing content.

In a Real Bank

The most respected junior bankers are those who are reliable under pressure. In the final 48 hours before a bid deadline, when the client CFO is anxious, the MD is unavailable, and three buyers are asking VDR questions simultaneously — the analyst who handles all of this calmly, accurately, and without escalating every small issue is worth significantly more to the team than one who produces technically brilliant models but creates chaos during execution.

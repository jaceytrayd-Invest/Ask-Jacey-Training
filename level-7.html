Level 07 · Derivatives & Structuring

Derivatives — Intuition Before Mathematics

Derivatives are instruments whose value derives from an underlying asset. They exist to transfer risk — from those who cannot bear it to those who will, for a price. This level builds intuition for options, futures, swaps, and structured instruments, and shows how they appear in real IB and lending contexts.

Featured Lesson — Options: Calls, Puts & Payoff Profiles

📍 Derivatives · Options · L7.02

Options are the most versatile derivative. Unlike forwards and futures — which create obligations — an option gives the holder a right without an obligation. This asymmetry is what makes options uniquely powerful: the buyer's downside is capped at the premium paid, while the upside is theoretically unlimited.

Key Option Terms

Strike price (K): The agreed price at which the underlying can be bought/sold Spot price (S): Current market price of the underlying Premium (P): Price paid for the option — the option's market value Expiry (T): Date on which the option expires — exercise or lapse Exercise style: European: Can only be exercised at expiry date American: Can be exercised any time up to and including expiry Moneyness: In the money (ITM): Call: S > K | Put: S < K (has intrinsic value) At the money (ATM): Call/Put: S ≈ K (intrinsic value ≈ zero) Out of the money (OTM): Call: S < K | Put: S > K (no intrinsic value) Option value = Intrinsic Value + Time Value Intrinsic value: max(S − K, 0) for a call; max(K − S, 0) for a put Time value: reflects probability that option becomes ITM before expiry

The Four Payoff Diagrams

Every option position has a payoff diagram — showing profit or loss at expiry for different prices of the underlying. These four are the building blocks from which all complex derivatives strategies are constructed.

Options in IB — Where They Actually Appear

Convertible bonds: A bond with an embedded call option on the issuer's equity. The bondholder has the right (not obligation) to convert to shares at the conversion price.

Employee stock options (ESOs): Call options granted to employees at a strike equal to the share price at grant date. The employee profits only if the stock rises — aligning incentives.

M&A deal protection: Option-like structures in deal agreements — break fees, reverse break fees, lockup agreements. A break fee paid if the deal fails is economically like a put option on the deal proceeding.

Caps, floors, collars on interest rates: An interest rate cap is a call option on interest rates — the borrower pays a premium to cap their floating rate exposure at a ceiling level.

The Most Common Options Misconception

Many students confuse the option's intrinsic value with its total value. An option always has some time value as long as time remains before expiry — even if it is out of the money. A call with S = £18 and K = £20 (OTM) has zero intrinsic value today, but still has positive time value because there is a chance S rises above £20 before expiry. As expiry approaches, time value decays to zero (theta decay) — and on expiry day, option value equals intrinsic value only.

In a Real Bank

IBD analysts rarely price options directly — that is the domain of the derivatives desk. But IBD professionals must understand options conceptually because they appear constantly: in convertible bond analysis (the embedded call option value drives the lower coupon), in M&A structuring (collar agreements, CVRs, contingent consideration), in equity compensation (option expensing under IFRS 2), and in financing structures (interest rate caps and floors on leveraged loans). The question is never "can you price Black-Scholes" — it is "do you understand what the option is doing in this structure and why."

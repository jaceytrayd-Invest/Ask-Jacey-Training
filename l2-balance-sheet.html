The Balance Sheet

The balance sheet is a snapshot of financial position at a single point in time. It shows what the business owns, what it owes, and what belongs to shareholders. One rule governs everything on it: Assets must always equal Liabilities plus Equity — always, without exception.

The Structure

The balance sheet has two sides that must always reconcile. The left side (Assets) shows resources. The right side (Liabilities + Equity) shows how those resources are financed.

ASSETS LIABILITIES + EQUITY ──────────────────────── ────────────────────────────── Current Assets: Current Liabilities: Cash & Equivalents Accounts Payable Accounts Receivable Short-term Debt / Revolver Inventory Accrued Liabilities Prepaid Expenses Deferred Revenue Current portion of LT debt Non-Current Assets: Property, Plant & Equipment Non-Current Liabilities: (net of depreciation) Long-term Debt Intangible Assets (net) Deferred Tax Liabilities Goodwill Lease Liabilities Investments Shareholders' Equity: Share Capital Retained Earnings Other Reserves ──────────────────────── ────────────────────────────── TOTAL ASSETS = TOTAL LIABILITIES + EQUITY

Current vs Non-Current — the liquidity cut

Assets and liabilities are split into current (due or convertible within 12 months) and non-current (longer than 12 months). This distinction matters for liquidity analysis — the current ratio (current assets / current liabilities) measures the company's ability to meet short-term obligations.

Goodwill — where acquisitions leave their mark

Goodwill is created when a company acquires another for more than the fair value of its net identifiable assets. It sits on the balance sheet as a non-current intangible asset. Goodwill is never amortised under IFRS — it is tested annually for impairment. A goodwill impairment charge hits the P&L and reduces equity. In a company with a heavy acquisition history, goodwill can be the largest single asset on the balance sheet — worth scrutinising carefully.

Goodwill = Purchase Price − Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets = (Consideration paid) − (Assets acquired − Liabilities assumed, at fair value)

The EV Bridge — why the Balance Sheet feeds valuation

Enterprise Value = Equity Value + Net Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred − Cash
The balance sheet provides all of these non-equity items. When you move from EV to equity value in a comps or DCF, you are reading the balance sheet. Every deal valuation requires an up-to-date balance sheet to execute the bridge. An analyst who misreads the balance sheet — mistaking gross debt for net debt, or missing off-balance-sheet leases — will produce a materially wrong valuation.

Common Mistakes

Using gross debt instead of net debt in the EV bridge. Net debt (debt minus cash) is the correct figure. If a company has £200m debt and £50m cash, net debt is £150m — not £200m. Using gross debt overstates the equity bridge by £50m.

Missing operating lease liabilities. Under IFRS 16, leases are capitalised on the balance sheet. The lease liability must be included in net debt calculations for EV purposes — many students forget this and understate leverage.

In a Real Bank

In due diligence, analysts build a "balance sheet normalisation" schedule — adjusting the reported balance sheet for deal-related items: refinancing existing debt, adding acquisition debt, adjusting for pension deficits, removing assets not being acquired (carve-out), and reflecting transaction fees. The pro-forma balance sheet at closing is what lenders and rating agencies assess, not the historical one.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

The balance sheet is our collateral map. Before lending, we identify every asset the borrower has that could secure our loan — and assess whether those assets retain value in a stress scenario. Receivables are usually high-quality collateral (quickly realisable). Inventory quality depends on whether it is finished goods or work-in-progress. PP&E is only good collateral if it has a liquid secondary market. Goodwill has zero collateral value. We then compute our loan-to-value ratio — total debt divided by realisable asset value — and ensure there is adequate coverage before we lend.

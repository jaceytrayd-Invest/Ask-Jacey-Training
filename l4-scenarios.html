📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.06 — Scenarios, Sensitivities & Model Output

A model without scenarios is an opinion. A model with scenarios is a tool. Scenario analysis converts a single-point estimate into an honest range of outcomes — and gives clients, lenders, and investors the information they need to make decisions under uncertainty.

Three Types of Analysis

TypeWhat it doesTypical use
Scenario analysisRe-runs the entire model under a different set of coherent assumptions (e.g., Base, Downside, Severe Downside)LBO returns analysis; credit stress testing; strategic planning
Sensitivity analysisIsolates the impact of changing one or two variables, holding everything else constantDCF WACC vs. terminal growth; LBO entry vs. exit multiple; margin vs. revenue growth
Tornado chartRanks all key assumptions by their impact on a single output (e.g., IRR), showing which variables matter mostIdentifying the key value drivers; prioritising management focus; risk communication

Building a Scenario Toggle

In the Inputs tab, create a single cell: SCENARIO (dropdown: 1=Base, 2=Downside, 3=Severe) For each assumption, use IF or CHOOSE to switch values: Revenue Growth Year 1: =CHOOSE(SCENARIO, Base_rev_growth, Down_rev_growth, Severe_rev_growth) e.g., =CHOOSE(SCENARIO, 8%, 3%, -5%) EBITDA Margin: =CHOOSE(SCENARIO, 35%, 31%, 26%) Capex % of Revenue: =CHOOSE(SCENARIO, 6%, 6%, 8%) ← stress capex higher in severe This means the entire model re-runs when you change SCENARIO cell from 1 to 2. No manual overrides. No risk of forgetting to update a cell. All scenarios always consistent.

The Summary Output Page — what MDs actually read

The output tab (or summary tab) is the first thing a senior banker reads. It should contain: the valuation summary (football field), key financial metrics in all three scenarios, leverage and cover ratios, the IRR and MoM matrix, and the sensitivity tables. Everything else in the model supports this page.

Design principles for the output page: no formulas visible, only values; no blue input cells; professional formatting that could be copied directly into a pitch book; all three scenarios visible on one screen without scrolling.

Communicating Uncertainty to Clients

Clients often want a single number: "What is it worth?" The honest answer is a range, with a central estimate and a clear explanation of what drives the spread. Never present a DCF output without a sensitivity table. Never present an LBO IRR without an entry vs. exit multiple matrix. The range is the answer — the base case is just the anchor point within it. A banker who says "the company is worth exactly £450m" without a range is either lying or has not tested their own assumptions.

Scenario Design Mistakes

Scenarios that are not internally consistent. A downside case where revenue falls 20% but margins stay flat ignores operating leverage. Revenue declines compress margins because fixed costs consume a larger share of lower revenue. If your downside margin assumption is identical to base, the scenario is not credible.

Cherry-picking sensitivity ranges. Running a WACC sensitivity from 9.0% to 10.0% instead of 8.0% to 12.0% artificially narrows the apparent range. Use realistic assumption bounds — the full range that an informed person might defensibly argue for, not just the range that makes the numbers comfortable.

In a Real Bank

Pitch book pages often feature two exhibits from the model: the football field (valuation range across methodologies) and an IRR sensitivity matrix (entry vs. exit multiple). These two pages summarise everything the senior client needs to make a decision. As an analyst, your job is to ensure these two pages are unimpeachable — every range is supported by the model, every assumption is documented, and the formatting is spotless. If the MD has to explain a number in the client meeting, you have failed. If every number on those two pages is self-explanatory, you have succeeded.

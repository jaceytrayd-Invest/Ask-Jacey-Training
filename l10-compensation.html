📌 Level 10 · Careers · L10.06 — Compensation — How IB Pay Works

IB compensation is high, variable, and often misunderstood. Understanding how base salary, year-end bonus, and deferred compensation interact — and how the bonus pool allocation process works — is essential for managing your own career economics and for having informed conversations about pay.

The Compensation Structure

Three components: 1. BASE SALARY (fixed, paid monthly): Set annually; varies by level and bank but relatively standardised across bulge brackets; increased significantly post-2021 across the industry UK analyst base: £65,000–£85,000 (bulge bracket, 2024 levels) UK associate base: £100,000–£130,000 UK VP base: £130,000–£175,000 UK Director base: £175,000–£250,000 UK MD base: £250,000–£400,000+ 2. YEAR-END BONUS (variable, paid January–March): Determined by: bank-wide performance, group performance, individual performance Analyst: £20,000–£80,000 (year 1–3 range; wide variation by bank and market) Associate: £50,000–£150,000 VP: £100,000–£350,000 Director: £150,000–£500,000 MD: £250,000–£2,000,000+ 3. DEFERRED COMPENSATION (above certain levels): Portions of bonus paid in bank stock or restricted units, vesting over 3–5 years Creates retention mechanism — leaving before vest date forfeits deferred comp Increasingly common above VP level; some banks impose deferred component for analysts

How the Bonus Pool Actually Works

Step 1: Bank-level pool determination Compensation committee reviews bank revenues and profit for the year Sets the total bonus pool as a % of revenues (typically 35–50% of revenues for front office in good years; significantly compressed in weak years) Step 2: Allocation to divisions Pool divided between IBD, S&T, Research, Asset Management based on their relative revenue contribution and strategic importance Step 3: Allocation within groups Group head allocates among their team — based on deal revenues, individual contribution, and the head's judgment about relative performance Step 4: Individual allocation Each individual receives a number based on their review In most banks, individuals are ranked within their class: Top bucket: maximum bonus (typically 1.5–2.5× average) Middle bucket: average bonus Lower bucket: below average (sometimes accompanied by "the talk") For analysts: the reviews are conducted by associates and VPs who worked with you; they feed into the formal performance review; the associate/VP's assessment drives the allocation more than anything else
The Bonus Conversation — How to Have It

Most analysts receive their bonus number in a brief conversation with their immediate supervisor or the group head — sometimes as short as two minutes. You will be told a number; sometimes but not always told how you ranked. After the meeting, you can ask questions — but the number is rarely negotiable at the analyst level. What you can legitimately ask: "How did my performance compare to the rest of the class?" and "Is there anything specific I should focus on to strengthen my next review?" These questions signal that you are engaged in your own development, not just your compensation.

In a Real Bank

The most reliable way to maximise your bonus is to be staffed on high-revenue deals with MDs who will advocate for you in the room where allocations are decided. Execution excellence is the baseline — above the baseline, the allocation is significantly shaped by who knows your name and what they associate it with. This is why seeking out senior bankers beyond your immediate deal team — presenting your work in broader team meetings, taking on cross-group projects — is both a development strategy and a compensation strategy.

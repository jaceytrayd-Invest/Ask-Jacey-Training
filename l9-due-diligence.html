📌 Level 09 · Deal Execution · L9.03 — Due Diligence — Financial & Commercial

Due diligence is the systematic verification of a target company's financial, legal, commercial, and operational profile before a transaction closes. It is where deals die, where prices are chipped, and where the seller's credibility is tested under conditions of maximum scrutiny. Understanding the workstreams and the dynamics is essential for both buy-side and sell-side bankers.

The QoE — the most consequential DD workstream

Quality of Earnings adjustment bridge: Reported EBITDA (statutory accounts): £45.0m + Non-recurring items excluded by management: £ 5.0m (restructuring costs) + Other management adjustments: £ 4.0m (share-based comp, forex) = Management Adjusted EBITDA: £54.0m ← seller's number QoE accountants then review each adjustment: − Restructuring: recurs annually (not truly non-recurring): (£3.5m) − Share-based comp: not added back in buyer's credit model: (£4.0m) − Customer: one customer (15% of revenue) gave notice to leave: (£6.0m) + IT efficiency: already achieved, conservative to include: £0.0m = QoE-Adjusted EBITDA: £40.5m ← buyer's number Gap: £54.0m seller vs. £40.5m buyer = 25% difference This gap directly translates into purchase price: at 12× EBITDA, £13.5m EBITDA difference = £162m lower acquisition price
What Breaks Deals in Due Diligence

Customer concentration risk — if one customer represents 25%+ of revenue and is flagged as at-risk during DD, buyers reprice or walk away. IP ownership gaps — software built on open-source code with licence violations, or IP held by a founder personally rather than the company, creates legal uncertainty that halts closing. Undisclosed litigation — if the QoE or legal team discovers material claims not flagged in the CIM, the seller's credibility collapses. Accounting irregularities — revenue pulled forward from future periods, costs deferred or reclassified — create both legal exposure and negotiating breakdown.

In a Real Bank

The sell-side bank does not conduct DD — they advise the seller on how to manage the DD process. This means: pre-empting QoE issues by commissioning vendor DD before the process; preparing management for the kinds of questions buyers will ask; making sure the data room is complete so buyers cannot claim they found "new" information; and managing the issues list (tracking every buyer concern, ensuring responses are timely and accurate). Deals that fail in DD almost always reflect either problems that were always there, or problems that were discovered late because the seller's preparation was inadequate.

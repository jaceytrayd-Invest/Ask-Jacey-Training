Featured Lesson — The Interview Process

📍 Careers · The Interview Process · L10.02

IB interviews are unlike most professional hiring processes. They test technical knowledge explicitly, assess behavioural fit under pressure, and are frequently conducted by people who were sitting where you are two or three years ago. Understanding the structure — and what each stage is really evaluating — is the foundation of effective preparation.

The Interview Structure

StageFormatWhat is being assessedTypical duration
1. CV screenApplication review by HR and junior bankersAcademic credentials, relevant experience, demonstrated interest in finance30 seconds per CV
2. First-round screen30-min video or phone call, usually with analyst or associateBasic technical competency (walk me through DCF), motivation, articulateness30 minutes
3. Technical testOnline or take-home modelling/accounting testNumerical accuracy, speed, accounting knowledge, attention to detail60–90 minutes
4. Superday / Assessment day3–6 interviews in one day with analysts, associates, VPs, and MDsTechnical depth, behavioural fit, pressure handling, commercial awarenessFull day
5. Case studyAnalyse a company and present to bankers (sometimes in-room, sometimes take-home)Analytical framework, quality of recommendations, presentation under pressure1–3 hours prep; 15 min presentation

Technical Interview — what you must know cold

GUARANTEED TECHNICAL QUESTIONS (prepare these first): Walk me through a DCF: → 5-year FCF forecast → WACC → terminal value (Gordon Growth or exit multiple) → Sum PV of FCFs + PV of terminal value = EV → minus net debt = equity value Walk me through the three financial statements: → P&L → CFS (indirect method) → BS → linkages between all three If net income increases by £100, how does it flow through all three statements? → P&L: NI up £100 → CFS: CFO up £100 (add-back cash) → BS: cash up £100, retained earnings up £100 → BS balances Walk me through an accretion/dilution analysis: → Cash deal: PF NI = Acquirer NI + Target NI − after-tax interest → Stock deal: PF NI = Acquirer NI + Target NI | PF shares = old + new issued → A/D% = (PF EPS − Standalone EPS) / Standalone EPS What is WACC and how do you calculate it? → WACC = (E/V × Re) + (D/V × Rd × (1−t)) → Re via CAPM: Rf + β(Rm−Rf) → Each component, its source, its purpose

Behavioural Interview — the STAR framework

Every behavioural question — "Tell me about a time you worked under pressure," "Describe a situation where you showed leadership" — should be answered using the STAR framework: Situation (context), Task (your responsibility), Action (what you specifically did), Result (what happened). The result should be quantified wherever possible.

Prepare three to five strong STAR stories from real experience — academic projects, internships, extracurricular leadership, part-time work. The same story can be adapted to answer multiple questions. The key: be specific. "I helped improve the team's performance" is weak. "I redesigned the shift scheduling system which reduced overtime costs by 18% and improved on-time delivery from 72% to 91%" is strong.

What Interviewers Are Actually Evaluating

Interviewers are asking three questions they will never say out loud: Is this person smart enough? (technical questions reveal this), Will this person work hard and do what is needed? (behavioural questions reveal this), and Do I want to sit next to this person at 2am during a deal crunch? (every moment of the interview reveals this). The third question is often the most decisive — and it is answered by how you handle the moments when you don't know the answer, how you respond to pushback, and whether you are someone the interviewer enjoys talking to.

In a Real Bank

The hiring decision is almost always made by consensus among everyone who interviewed the candidate. One enthusiastic advocate can sometimes save a candidate who had one weak interview; one strong objection from an MD can veto a candidate who performed well everywhere else. The practical implication: you are being assessed in every interaction — including the coffee with the analyst before the formal interviews begin, the elevator ride, and the email you send to thank people afterwards.

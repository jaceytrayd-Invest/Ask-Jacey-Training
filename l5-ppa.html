📌 Level 05 · M&A · L5.06 — Purchase Price Allocation (PPA)

Purchase Price Allocation is the accounting process that occurs after a deal closes — allocating the total acquisition price across the identifiable assets, liabilities, and intangibles of the acquired business. Its outputs create real, ongoing earnings impacts that most deal teams underestimate.

The PPA Framework (IFRS 3 / ASC 805)

Purchase Price = Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets + Goodwill Where: Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets = + Fair value of tangible assets (PP&E, inventory, receivables) + Fair value of identifiable intangible assets (separately valued): Customer relationships Technology / patents Brand / trademarks Non-compete agreements Licences and contracts − Fair value of liabilities assumed − Fair value of contingent liabilities identified Goodwill = Purchase Price − Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets = The unallocated premium (assembled workforce, strategic premium, growth optionality that cannot be separately valued)

PPA's Impact on Post-Deal P&L

PPA amortisation is a real, recurring P&L charge that reduces reported earnings for years after the deal. It is often ignored in deal models — but sophisticated acquirers build it in from day one.

Example — £400m acquisition: Net tangible assets at fair value: £120m Customer relationships (10yr): £90m → £9.0m/year amortisation Technology / IP (5yr): £60m → £12.0m/year amortisation Brand (20yr): £30m → £1.5m/year amortisation Non-compete (3yr): £15m → £5.0m/year amortisation Goodwill (residual): £85m → £0 (no amortisation; impairment only) ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────── Total: £400m Annual PPA amortisation: £9.0 + £12.0 + £1.5 + £5.0 = £27.5m After-tax (25%): £27.5m × 0.75 = £20.6m/year EPS reduction

Goodwill Impairment

Under IFRS, goodwill is not amortised but must be tested annually for impairment at the Cash Generating Unit (CGU) level. If the recoverable amount of the CGU falls below its carrying value (including goodwill), the difference is recognised as an impairment charge — reducing goodwill and hitting the income statement. Goodwill impairments are often the accounting acknowledgement that an acquisition overpaid or failed to deliver.

Why Analysts Must Understand PPA

In a pro forma accretion/dilution model, if you ignore PPA amortisation, you overstate pro forma earnings — making the deal look more accretive than it really is. In a DCF of the acquired business, PPA amortisation is non-cash and must be added back to derive FCF (similar to D&A). Knowing the difference between "reported EPS accretion" (post-PPA) and "cash EPS accretion" (pre-PPA) is a distinction sophisticated clients and investors always draw — and so should you.

In a Real Bank

PPA is performed post-close by a specialist valuation team (typically Big 4 or specialist intangible asset valuers). The bank's role is to anticipate the PPA charges in the pro forma model before the deal closes — so the board and investors know what the reported earnings will look like in Years 1–5. Getting this wrong can mean a deal that was presented as "2% accretive" turns out to be dilutive once PPA amortisation is applied — damaging the bank's credibility and the deal team's reputation.

Lesson Preview — Capital Structure

📍 Foundations · Capital Structure · L1.03

Capital structure is the answer to one question: how does a company fund itself? Every deal — M&A, IPO, LBO — involves deciding or disrupting a capital structure. Understanding it is non-negotiable.

The Hierarchy of Claims

Capital providers are ranked by their claim on the company's assets and cash flows. This ranking is called seniority. Senior creditors get paid first. Equity shareholders get paid last — but take the most upside if things go well.

PositionInstrumentClaim TypeTypical ReturnRisk
1stSenior Secured Debt (1st Lien)Fixed interest, principal repaymentSOFR + 350–550bpsLowest
2ndSenior Unsecured / 2nd LienFixed interest, sub. to senior6–9%Low–medium
3rdSubordinated / High Yield BondsFixed coupon, junior claim8–13%Medium
4thMezzanine / PIKHybrid — debt economics, equity exposure13–18%High
5thPreferred EquityDividend before common, limited upside8–12%High
LastCommon EquityResidual — all upside after debt paid15–25%+ targetHighest
Key Principle

Risk and return are inversely related to seniority. Senior lenders take the least risk and accept the lowest return. Equity investors take the most risk but capture all the upside once debt is repaid. This is why PE firms earn 20–30% target returns — they sit at the very bottom of the stack.

Enterprise Value vs. Equity Value

Enterprise Value (EV) is the total value of the business — available to all capital providers (debt and equity). Equity Value is what's left for shareholders after all debt claims are satisfied.

Enterprise Value (EV) = Equity Value + Net Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred
Or equivalently:
Equity Value = EV − Net Debt − Minority Interest − Preferred

Net Debt = Total Debt − Cash & Equivalents
Common Mistake

Confusing EV and equity value in a comps analysis. EV multiples (EV/EBITDA) are capital-structure neutral — use them to compare companies with different leverage profiles. P/E and other equity-level multiples are capital-structure dependent — they're distorted by leverage and tax. Always be clear which you're using and why.

Why This Matters in Every Deal

In M&A: the acquirer pays EV. The equity valuation comes after the EV-to-equity bridge. In an LBO: the PE firm levers up the capital structure to amplify equity returns. In DCF: you build to EV first, then subtract debt to reach equity value. In IPOs: new shares represent incremental equity — but EV stays the same if cash raised stays in the business.

In a Real Bank

The EV bridge is the first thing a junior analyst builds when assigned a new company. MD asks "what's the company worth?" — you can't answer without knowing EV, net debt position, minority interests, and shares outstanding. Any confusion here in a pitch meeting is immediately visible to clients.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

From a private credit perspective, capital structure analysis is how we assess where we sit in the waterfall. A senior secured lender at 4× Net Debt/EBITDA has a fundamentally different risk profile than a 2nd lien lender at 6.5×. The "equity cushion" below us — the amount of value the company can lose before we take losses — is the primary credit metric we monitor. Equity value = our first line of protection.

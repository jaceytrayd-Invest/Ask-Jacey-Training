📌 Level 03 · Valuation · L3.06 — Terminal Value & Sensitivity Analysis

Terminal value is where most DCF value lives — and where most DCF error originates. Sensitivity analysis transforms a single-point DCF output into a defensible range. Both are mandatory before presenting a DCF to any client.

Terminal Value — Two Methods

Gordon Growth Model (Perpetuity Growth)

TV = FCFn × (1 + g) / (WACC - g) Inputs: FCFn = Free cash flow in the final forecast year g = Perpetuity growth rate (long-run sustainable growth) WACC = Discount rate Constraint: WACC must exceed g — otherwise the denominator is negative Typical g: 1.5-3.0% for developed market businesses (anchored to long-run nominal GDP growth) Higher for emerging markets or secular growth sectors

Exit Multiple Method

TV = EBITDAn × Exit EV/EBITDA Multiple Inputs: EBITDAn = EBITDA in the final forecast year Exit Multiple = EV/EBITDA at which you expect to sell the business Where the multiple comes from: - Current peer trading multiples (assume mean reversion) - Historical transaction multiples in the sector - Conservative: apply a 10-20% discount to current multiples

Choosing Between the Two Methods

Gordon GrowthExit Multiple
Best forStable, mature businesses with predictable cash flowsMost M&A contexts; businesses with active transaction markets
AdvantageTheoretically pure; no assumption about future market pricesGrounded in observable market multiples; more intuitive
RiskExtremely sensitive to g — small changes cause large TV movesAssumes current multiples persist; circular if market is mispriced

The Two-Way Sensitivity Table

Always present a sensitivity table with WACC and terminal growth rate (or exit multiple) as the two axes. This converts a single-point estimate into a range of outcomes — and gives the client and reader a sense of how assumption changes move the valuation.

Example: EV Sensitivity Table (£m) Terminal Growth Rate WACC 1.0% 1.5% 2.0% 2.5% 3.0% 7.5% 542 578 620 671 734 8.0% 498 528 562 603 651 8.5% 459 486 515 549 590 9.0% 425 448 473 501 534 ← Base case 9.5% 394 414 436 460 488 10.0% 367 384 402 423 446 Base case (9.0% WACC, 2.0% growth) = £473m Range (table extremes): £367m – £734m (nearly 2x spread)
Present the Table, Not Just the Base Case

A DCF that presents only one EV number is misleading — it implies a precision that does not exist. The sensitivity table is the honest version of the DCF. Clients should understand that the base case is not a prediction; it is the central estimate within a defensible range. The range is the output; the base case is just the midpoint.

Terminal Value Warning Signs

TV as more than 90% of EV: Usually signals the explicit forecast period is too short, or FCFs in years 1–5 are too low (possibly incorrect). Extend the explicit forecast or review near-term assumptions.

Implied perpetuity growth back-solve above GDP: If your exit multiple method implies g of 5%+ in a mature industry, either the exit multiple is too high or the EBITDA is understated. No developed-market business can grow faster than the economy in perpetuity.

Not stress-testing the WACC: Every single DCF must show what happens at WACC ± 1.5%. If you do not have this table, do not present the DCF to an MD.

In a Real Bank

In a fairness opinion for a public company acquisition, the financial advisor must justify the valuation range in writing. The sensitivity tables are the primary defence — they show that even under conservative assumptions, the deal price is within the range of fair value. Courts and regulators scrutinise these tables carefully. In one well-known case, a financial advisor faced regulatory action because their sensitivity table had "cherry-picked" WACC and growth assumptions that conveniently made the deal price look attractive.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We run our own sensitivity analysis before every credit — not on EV, but on FCF. We test: at what EBITDA decline does the borrower breach their interest cover covenant? At what level does FCF turn negative? Our "covenant headroom" analysis is essentially a sensitivity table showing how much room the borrower has before they breach. If headroom is less than 15% EBITDA decline, we treat the credit as high-watch from inception.

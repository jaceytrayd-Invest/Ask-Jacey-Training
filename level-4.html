Featured Lesson — The 3-Statement Model

📍 Modelling · 3-Statement Model · L4.02

The 3-statement model is the foundation of all IB financial modelling. Every DCF, LBO, and M&A model is built on top of it. If the three statements do not link correctly, nothing downstream is reliable.

The Build Sequence

Never build the three statements simultaneously. Build them in this specific order — each feeds the next:

StepWhat you buildWhat it produces
1Income Statement (P&L)Net Income — the starting line of the CFS, and the input to retained earnings
2Cash Flow Statement (top half)Cash Flow from Operations — using net income + D&A + working capital changes
3Debt ScheduleEnding debt balances, interest expense, mandatory repayments — feeds back to P&L interest line
4Cash Flow Statement (full)CFI (capex, acquisitions) + CFF (debt, equity, dividends) = net change in cash
5Balance SheetBuilt from all the above — must balance as the final check

The Circularity Problem

The most important — and most commonly mishandled — feature of a 3-statement model is the circularity between interest expense and cash. Interest expense depends on the debt balance, which depends on how much cash you have to repay debt, which depends on net income after interest, which depends on interest expense. This is a circular reference.

The circle: Interest Expense → lowers Net Income → lowers CFO → lowers ending cash → affects debt repayment → changes debt balance → changes Interest Expense Two approaches to handle circularity: 1. Enable iterative calculations in Excel (File → Options → Formulas → Enable Iterative) — Excel solves the loop automatically after multiple passes 2. Use a circularity switch (a helper column that breaks the loop manually) — More transparent; easier to audit; preferred in professional models

The Balance Check — your error signal

The balance sheet must balance: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. In a well-built model, you add a check row at the bottom of the balance sheet:

Balance Check = Total Assets - (Total Liabilities + Total Equity) Should always equal: £0.00 If it does NOT equal zero: → One of the five statement linkages is broken → Retained earnings is not rolling forward correctly → Cash is not being correctly updated from the CFS → A debt or capex balance sheet line is not connected to the right schedule Colour code the check cell: green (=0) or red (≠0) using conditional formatting. Never send a model to a client with a red balance check — ever.
The Non-Negotiable Model Discipline

Hard-code nothing inside a formula. Every assumption — revenue growth rate, margin %, tax rate, capex as % of revenue — lives in a clearly labelled input cell, coloured blue (inputs are always blue in professional models). Formulas reference input cells. If a number is ever typed directly into a formula, you cannot change the assumption without hunting through hundreds of cells. This is how model errors stay hidden for months.

The Four Build Errors That Sink Models

1. Not balancing the balance sheet. If you skip the balance check, errors accumulate silently. Make it your last formula, built on day one.

2. Mixing input years and forecast years. Historical data goes in clearly labelled columns; forecast columns start with a visual break. Mixing them creates formula errors that are nearly invisible.

3. Forgetting the deferred tax movement. Timing differences between accounting profit and taxable profit create deferred tax assets/liabilities that must balance sheet balance. Omitting them breaks the BS every time.

4. Not handling the cash sweep correctly. In leveraged models, excess cash after debt service is swept to repay revolving credit or accelerate amortisation. Getting the order of operations wrong makes debt balances wrong, which makes interest wrong, which makes everything wrong.

In a Real Bank

Investment banks maintain proprietary model templates for each product — M&A, LBO, DCF, merger model. First-year analysts are trained on these templates in the first weeks of their programme. The templates enforce structure, colour coding, and formatting conventions. Your job as an analyst is to adapt the template to the specific deal, not to rebuild from scratch. Models are shared across teams and must be immediately legible to any senior banker who opens them at 11pm before a board meeting.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

When we receive a financial model from a borrower or their advisors, the first thing we do is stress-test it — not run it at base case. We change the revenue growth assumption to zero, then negative. We compress EBITDA margins by 300–500bps. We shock interest rates up 200bps. We check whether the model balance sheet still balances under all these scenarios. A model that breaks under stress scenarios tells us the advisor built it around the base case — not as a genuine planning tool. We take that as a yellow flag on the quality of diligence overall.

Featured Lesson — The 3-Statement Model

📍 Modelling · The 3-Statement Model · L4.03

The 3-statement model is the foundation of everything in IB. Every LBO model, every DCF, every merger model is built on top of a functioning 3-statement model. Before you build anything more complex, you must be able to build this cleanly, fast, and correctly — every time.

The Tab Architecture

A professional 3-statement model separates inputs from calculations from outputs. The standard tab structure:

TabContentsColour convention
CoverDeal name, company, date, version, analyst nameDark background, branded
AssumptionsAll hardcoded inputs — revenue growth, margins, capex, WC days, tax rateBlue/green background (inputs)
Income StatementHistorical actuals + forecast P&L, all driven by assumptions tabWhite background
Balance SheetHistorical actuals + forecast BS, fully linked to IS and CFSWhite background
Cash Flow StatementDerived from IS and BS — reconciles net change in cashWhite background
Debt ScheduleAmortisation, PIK, RCF, cash sweep — feeds interest to IS and debt to BSWhite or grey
OutputValuation summary, returns, charts — formatted for client presentationClean, printable

The Build Sequence

Build in this exact order. Skipping steps or building out of sequence creates errors that are extremely hard to find later.

Step 1: Historical actuals → Input 3 years of historical IS, BS, and CFS data Step 2: Income Statement forecast → Drive from assumption tab Revenue → Gross Profit → EBITDA → EBIT → EBT → Net Income Step 3: Debt Schedule → Calculate interest expense and debt movements (feeds back to IS — creates circularity, handle with iteration) Step 4: Cash Flow Statement → Start with Net Income + D&A (from IS) ± WC changes (from BS delta) − Capex → CFO CFI: Capex, acquisitions CFF: Debt drawdown/repayment, dividends, equity = Net change in cash Step 5: Balance Sheet → Drive from IS + CFS Cash = prior cash + net change in cash (from CFS) PP&E = prior PP&E + Capex − D&A Retained Earnings = prior RE + Net Income − Dividends Debt = from Debt Schedule Step 6: Balance check → Assets must equal Liabilities + Equity If not: one of the five linkages is broken

The Circularity Problem

Interest expense depends on debt balance. Debt balance depends on free cash flow (which goes to repay debt). Free cash flow is after interest (a cost). This creates a circular reference — interest feeds into cash flow which affects debt which determines interest.

The professional solution: enable Excel's iterative calculation (File → Options → Formulas → Enable Iterative Calculation, set max iterations to 100). This allows Excel to resolve the circularity by repeatedly recalculating until the values converge. Most professional IB models use this approach rather than breaking the circularity with a "hard-coded" interest rate assumption.

The Balance Sheet Check — Your Integrity Test

Add a row at the bottom of your balance sheet: CHECK = Total Assets − (Total Liabilities + Total Equity). This must equal exactly zero in every period. If it does not, something is wrong in the linkages. Format this cell to show red if not zero. The balance sheet check is the most important formula in the model — it tells you the model is mechanically correct.

The Errors That Sink Models

Hardcoding a number where a formula should be. If revenue in Year 3 is typed as £220m rather than linked to an assumption, changing the growth rate assumption won't update it. Every cell in the forecast should be a formula — never a hardcode.

Using the wrong sign convention. Capex in CFS is typically shown as a negative (cash out). If you accidentally enter it as a positive, the model treats capex as a cash inflow. Consistency of sign convention throughout the model is essential.

Not including the opening balance in balance sheet rolls. PP&E (end) = PP&E (start) + Capex − D&A. If you forget the opening balance, the balance sheet will be wrong from Year 1 onwards and the error compounds every year.

In a Real Bank

IB models are living documents — they are updated every time new information arrives. An analyst might update a model 15–20 times during a deal process. Keeping the model clean (no hardcodes, consistent structure, all links traceable) means that updates take 30 minutes rather than 3 hours. The analyst who builds a clean, flexible model saves everyone enormous time over the life of a deal — and that capability is noticed.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

When we receive a 3-statement model from a borrower or their advisor, we run our own audit immediately. We look for: hardcoded numbers in the forecast (suggests the model was built to produce a specific answer), balance sheet checks (does it balance?), interest calculations (are they using beginning or average debt balance?), and working capital assumptions (are DSO/DIO/DPO realistic vs. actuals?). A model that fails these checks tells us something about how the deal is being presented — and we probe harder in due diligence.

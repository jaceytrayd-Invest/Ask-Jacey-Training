📌 Level 10 · Careers · L10.05 — Exit Opportunities — Where IB Bankers Go

The IB analyst experience is widely regarded as one of the best training grounds in finance. The skills are directly applicable to a range of roles in private markets, investing, and corporate strategy. Understanding the exit landscape — who hires, what they value, and how recruitment works — enables deliberate career planning rather than reactive job searching.

Private Equity Recruitment — how it actually works

ON-CYCLE RECRUITMENT (megafunds and upper mid-market): When: months 6–18 of analyst programme — sometimes as early as month 4 Initiated by: headhunters (placement firms) who approach analysts directly Timeline: 1-3 weeks from first contact to offer — extremely compressed Process: headhunter call → PE fund screening → LBO test → partner interviews LBO test: typically 3-hour paper LBO modelling exercise Common format: build a simple LBO, calculate IRR, assess credit metrics, write a 1-page investment recommendation OFF-CYCLE RECRUITMENT (smaller funds, sector-focused PE): When: throughout the year, less structured timeline Initiated by: direct approach, LinkedIn, headhunter, networking Timeline: 2-4 months typically Process: more varied — sometimes includes deal case studies, sector theses Advantage: less competition, more time to prepare, more scope for fit WHAT PE FIRMS ASSESS: Technical: can you build an LBO model accurately and quickly? Judgment: does your investment recommendation show good instincts? Work experience: have you worked on deals, not just pitched them? Fit: are you someone the partners will want to work closely with for 5+ years?
Corporate Development — the Underrated Exit

Many analysts overlook corporate development (CorpDev) at large companies as an exit option — undervaluing it because it lacks the prestige of PE and the earning potential of a top hedge fund. In practice, senior CorpDev roles at major companies are intellectually demanding, strategically interesting, and often provide a direct path to CFO or COO over a 10–15 year arc. The analyst at a consumer company working on their buy-and-build strategy is doing genuinely important work — and often has more direct impact on business outcomes than a junior PE associate reviewing portcos from the outside.

In a Real Bank

Banks are ambivalent about analysts leaving for PE — they know it happens and have largely accepted it as part of the analyst value proposition. What banks genuinely do not want: analysts who are visibly disengaged because they have already secured a PE offer and are coasting through the last six months of their programme. Analysts who maintain full engagement and contribution through their final day earn genuine references, alumni network access, and the goodwill of the MDs who may appear as LPs in the PE funds they eventually join.

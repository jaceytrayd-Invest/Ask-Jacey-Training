📌 Level 00 — Orientation · L0.04 — The Analyst's Workflow

What does a first-year IB analyst actually do all day? The reality is more structured than the mythology suggests — and more repetitive. Understanding the workflow before you join is the difference between surviving the first six months and excelling in them.

A Typical Day (and Night)

There is no typical day — but there is a typical range. Analysts typically arrive at 9–10am (or earlier if markets are open and relevant), and leave anywhere from 10pm on a quiet night to 3–4am during a live pitch or deal crunch. The work is never linear — a "just a quick question" from an MD at 7pm can turn into three additional hours of analysis.

Analyst time allocation (rough average): Modelling and analysis: ~35% Pitch book formatting: ~30% Data gathering / comps: ~15% Coordination / admin: ~10% Client/meeting prep: ~10% Hours per week: 80–100 in a busy period; 60–70 in lighter periods Total annual hours: approximately 3,500–4,500

Tools you will use every day

Excel: Primary modelling tool. Shortcuts, formula hygiene, and model structure are the first things juniors are judged on. Practice Excel speed until keyboard shortcuts are automatic.

PowerPoint: Pitch books, management presentations, CIMs. Formatting to pixel-perfect precision is a real skill — and one MDs care deeply about.

Bloomberg Terminal: Real-time market data, comps financials, bond pricing, equity research. A Bloomberg certification is useful pre-joining.

CapIQ / Capital IQ: Financial data, M&A deal databases, screening tools. The primary source for comps and precedent transactions.

Mergermarket / Dealogic: M&A deal intelligence — useful for precedent transaction research and league table data.

The Most Important Skill No One Mentions

Managing up. Your ability to understand exactly what an MD or Director needs, deliver it without being asked twice, and flag problems before they become crises is what distinguishes good analysts from great ones. Technical skills are table stakes. The ability to manage the workflow, communicate clearly, and make your seniors' lives easier is what gets you promoted.

In a Real Bank

First-year analysts at bulge-bracket banks (Goldman, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, Barclays, HSBC) in London typically earn £65–85k base salary plus a bonus of £40–80k in Year 1 — depending on the bank and deal flow. Elite boutiques (Lazard, Rothschild, Evercore) pay comparably. All-in comp of £100–160k in Year 2–3 is realistic for good performers. The quid pro quo: those hours.

📌 Level 08 · Private Markets · L8.03 — IRR, MOIC & Return Attribution

Return measurement is the language of private equity. IRR and MOIC are the two primary metrics — and they tell different stories about the same investment. Understanding how they are calculated, how they diverge, and how to attribute returns to their sources is analytical mastery that distinguishes strong candidates.

IRR vs. MOIC — two lenses on returns

IRR (Internal Rate of Return)MOIC (Money on Money Multiple)
DefinitionAnnualised return, accounting for timing of cash flowsTotal return multiple — ratio of cash out to cash in
FormulaDiscount rate where NPV = 0Total proceeds / Total invested capital
Considers timingYes — early returns valued higherNo — £1 returned in Year 1 same as Year 10
Considers magnitudePartially — penalises long holdsFully — £200m return > £100m regardless of timing
Who weights itLPs comparing managers; reinvestment opportunity costLPs tracking absolute wealth creation (DPI)
Gaming riskCan be inflated by early distributions (dividend recaps)Cannot be inflated — only measures total proceeds

IRR Approximation — quick calculation

For a simple one-period LBO (invest at Year 0, exit at Year N): IRR = MOIC^(1/N) - 1 Examples: 2.0x in 3 years: IRR = 2.0^(1/3) - 1 = 1.260 - 1 = 26.0% 2.5x in 4 years: IRR = 2.5^(1/4) - 1 = 1.257 - 1 = 25.7% 3.0x in 5 years: IRR = 3.0^(1/5) - 1 = 1.246 - 1 = 24.6% 3.0x in 7 years: IRR = 3.0^(1/7) - 1 = 1.170 - 1 = 17.0% 4.0x in 5 years: IRR = 4.0^(1/5) - 1 = 1.319 - 1 = 31.9% Key observation: the same 3.0x MOIC generates 24.6% IRR in 5 years but only 17.0% in 7 years. Holding time dramatically affects IRR while leaving MOIC unchanged. This is why PE firms prioritise exits that maximise IRR — sometimes selling earlier than optimal for absolute value creation.

Return Attribution — decomposing the gain

Three-part attribution framework: EBITDA Growth Contribution: = (Exit EBITDA - Entry EBITDA) × Entry EV/EBITDA multiple Values the earnings improvement at entry market rates Multiple Expansion Contribution: = (Exit multiple - Entry multiple) × Exit EBITDA Values the re-rating effect on exit-year earnings Debt Paydown Contribution: = Entry Debt - Exit Debt The direct equity benefit of debt reduction during hold Check: Entry Equity + EBITDA Growth + Multiple Expansion + Debt Paydown = Exit Equity Proceeds
Portfolio-Level vs. Deal-Level Metrics

Deal-level IRR tells you how a single investment performed. Fund-level IRR tells you the blended return across all investments in the fund — which is what LPs actually receive. The difference can be large: a fund might have five great deals (30%+ IRR each) and three disasters (0.5x or worse). The fund-level IRR depends on how much capital was deployed in each deal and the timing of each. Top-quartile PE funds typically deliver net-of-fees fund IRRs of 20–25%; median funds deliver 12–18%.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

Our return metric is simpler than PE's: we target a gross IRR of 9–13% on each loan, depending on the risk profile. This is composed of: interest income (SOFR + margin), OID amortisation, and commitment/monitoring fees. We do not have MOIC targets — our capital returns 1.0x at par at maturity (plus interim coupon income). The risk is not that we earn less than expected — it is that we do not get par back (credit loss). Our job is to size loans such that the recovery value in a distressed scenario still exceeds our outstanding balance. This is fundamentally different from the PE equity mentality.

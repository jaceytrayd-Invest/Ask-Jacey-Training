Featured Lesson — Restructuring & Distressed Situations

📍 Advanced · Restructuring · L11.01

Restructuring is the discipline that applies when a company's obligations exceed its ability to pay them. It is the most intellectually demanding corner of IB — combining financial analysis, legal mechanics, negotiation strategy, and human judgment about when to push and when to give. Every lender, every creditor, and every large corporate finance professional needs to understand this.

The Distress Spectrum

StageWhat it meansOptions available
Liquidity stressShort-term cash shortage — company cannot meet near-term obligations but is fundamentally solvent (assets > liabilities)RCF draw, covenant waiver, asset sales, emergency equity injection, bridge loan
Covenant breachFinancial covenants (leverage, cover) breached — technical default but not cash default. Lender has acceleration rights but not yet exercisingWaiver and reset negotiation, margin step-up, equity cure, amendment and extend
Balance sheet insolvencyLiabilities exceed assets at fair market value — the business is worth less than it owes. Debt is trading below par.Out-of-court restructuring, debt-for-equity swap, scheme of arrangement, administration
Cash flow insolvencyCompany cannot pay obligations as they fall due — imminent payment defaultFormal insolvency process (administration, Chapter 11), pre-pack, liquidation

The Restructuring Toolkit

OUT-OF-COURT RESTRUCTURING (preferred — faster, cheaper, confidential): Amendment and Extend (A&E): Loan maturity extended; interest rate stepped up; new covenants agreed Requires lender consent — unanimous or per threshold in loan docs (typically 66.7%) Advantages: no court involvement; preserves confidentiality; fastest Risk: holdout creditors — those who do not agree can block Debt-for-Equity Swap: Creditors exchange debt claims for equity in the company Appropriate when: enterprise value < total debt (equity already worthless) Senior creditors become the new equity owners; existing equity wiped out Requires valuation of the business to determine conversion ratio Partial debt repurchase / tender offer: Company buys back its own distressed debt at below-par prices Reduces debt burden and generates an accounting gain Requires sufficient liquidity — typically needs new money alongside FORMAL INSOLVENCY (when out-of-court fails or is not possible): UK Administration: Court-appointed administrator takes control; moratorium on creditor action Administrator's priority: rescue as going concern > sale > winddown Pre-pack: asset sale agreed before administration; announced on appointment Speed: faster than US Chapter 11; typical 6-18 months US Chapter 11: Debtor-in-possession (DIP) structure: management retains control initially Automatic stay on all creditor claims — breathing space for reorganisation Plan of Reorganisation (PoR) agreed between debtor and creditor classes Cram-down: can bind dissenting creditor classes if absolute priority satisfied Timeline: typically 12-24 months; pre-negotiated (pre-pack) can be faster

The Priority Waterfall in Distress

In any restructuring or insolvency, proceeds are distributed in strict priority order. Understanding the waterfall determines who recovers what — and therefore who has negotiating leverage.

Enterprise Value at Recovery (what the business is worth in distress) Distributed in this order: 1. Administration/restructuring costs and professional fees [paid first] 2. DIP financing (new money provided during the process) [super senior] 3. Super-senior RCF (if super-senior in capital structure) 4. Senior Secured First Lien (TLB) [typically par recovery if sufficient EV] 5. Senior Secured Second Lien 6. Senior Unsecured (HY bonds, trade creditors) 7. Subordinated / Mezzanine debt 8. Preferred equity 9. Common equity [typically wiped out] The "fulcrum security" is the debt class where enterprise value runs out. → Creditors above the fulcrum: recover at par (or close) → Creditors at the fulcrum: receive partial recovery (some cash, some equity) → Creditors below: receive little or nothing The fulcrum security holders have the most negotiating power in a restructuring — they are the ones who will own the reorganised company and have the most to gain or lose from the outcome.
The JaceyTrayd Perspective on Distressed Credits

When one of our portfolio credits enters stress, our objectives are precisely ordered: first, protect par recovery; second, preserve the ongoing lending relationship and brand; third, maximise recovery beyond par if circumstances allow. We do not try to become equity investors in a distressed situation unless forced. Our loan agreements are designed to give us early warning (maintenance covenants), adequate security (first-ranking charge over all assets), and equity cure mechanisms that allow the PE sponsor to fix problems before we have to enforce. When enforcement becomes necessary, we move quickly — extended uncertainty is more damaging to recovery value than decisive action.

In a Real Bank — Restructuring Advisory

Restructuring advisory is a specialised IB product. Dedicated restructuring groups at banks (Houlihan Lokey, Lazard, Rothschild, Moelis are the leading independents; Goldman, JPMorgan, and Evercore have strong internal practices) advise either the distressed company (debtor-side) or the creditor group (creditor-side). The work is intellectually intense — modelling enterprise value in a distress context, understanding priority disputes, advising on negotiation strategy — and counter-cyclical (busier in recessions, when most IB revenue falls). Restructuring bankers are among the most technically sophisticated in the industry.

📌 Level 11 · Advanced · L11.04 — ESG in Finance — From Reporting to Pricing

ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) has moved from a reporting obligation to a financial variable that affects pricing, credit analysis, and deal structuring. Understanding the instruments, the frameworks, and the genuine risks — and being able to distinguish substance from greenwashing — is now a core IB competency.

TCFD — the disclosure framework

Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) Four pillars of recommended disclosure: 1. GOVERNANCE → How the board oversees climate-related risks and opportunities → Management's role in assessing and managing climate risk 2. STRATEGY → How climate risks and opportunities affect strategy, financial planning → Scenario analysis: physical risk (2°C and 4°C warming scenarios) and transition risk (orderly vs disorderly decarbonisation) 3. RISK MANAGEMENT → How the organisation identifies, assesses, and manages climate risk → Integration into overall enterprise risk management 4. METRICS AND TARGETS → Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (purchased energy), Scope 3 (value chain) emissions → Carbon targets and progress against them → Climate-related financial impacts where material UK Mandatory: Large UK companies and financial institutions must disclose TCFD-aligned information — this is now regulatory, not voluntary.

ESG in Credit Analysis

ESG factors affect credit quality through two distinct risk channels:

Risk typeWhat it meansCredit impactExamples
Physical riskDirect damage from climate events — floods, fires, storms — that can impair assets, interrupt operations, or reduce collateral valueIncreased expected loss on property-secured loans; operational disruption; supply chain riskCoastal property exposed to sea-level rise; manufacturing in flood-prone areas
Transition riskPolicy, technology, and market changes associated with the transition to a low-carbon economy — carbon pricing, stranded assets, consumer preference shiftsEBITDA margin compression; stranded asset write-downs; regulatory compliance costsOil & gas producer facing carbon tax; coal miner with stranded reserves; internal combustion vehicle manufacturer
Greenwashing — Protecting Credibility

Greenwashing undermines the ESG market's credibility and creates reputational and regulatory risk for lenders. The three tests JaceyTrayd applies to every SLL structure: Materiality — do the KPIs relate to the company's most significant environmental impacts, not peripheral activities? Ambition — do the targets represent genuine improvement beyond business-as-usual trajectory? Verification — is performance independently verified by a credible third party under a recognised standard (ISAE 3000)?

🔑 JaceyTrayd — ESG in Our Credit Process

We have embedded ESG assessment into our standard credit underwriting. For every new credit: (1) we assess physical climate risk in the geographic footprint (particularly for asset-backed credits where collateral is exposed to flood or fire risk); (2) we assess transition risk for carbon-intensive industries — oil and gas, cement, steel, aviation — modelling EBITDA impact under a carbon price of £50, £100, and £150 per tonne; (3) we prefer SLL structures that create ongoing financial incentives for ESG improvement. We do not exclude carbon-intensive industries categorically — but we price the transition risk explicitly in our margin and covenant package.

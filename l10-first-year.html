📌 Level 10 · Careers · L10.03 — The First Year — Surviving and Thriving

The first year of an IB analyst programme is the steepest learning curve in professional finance. The hours are long, the feedback is direct, and the standard is high. What is less often discussed: the specific behaviours that distinguish analysts who are kept, promoted, and respected from those who struggle.

The Reality of the Analyst Experience

DimensionWhat to expectWhat to do about it
Hours70–100 hours/week during live deal periods; 50–70 in quieter weeks. Banking hours are real and non-negotiable during deal executionBuild physical and mental systems for sustained performance — sleep habits, exercise, nutrition. Unsustainable short-term intensity requires sustainable long-term recovery habits
FeedbackDirect, frequent, often delivered at 11pm by email after reviewing your work. Tone varies by bank and individual senior bankerTreat all feedback as information, not judgment. The fastest way to improve is to implement feedback completely and then ask "is this now what you were looking for?"
Type of workMonths 1–6: mostly formatting, data gathering, model building to spec. Months 7–18: increasing analytical judgment. Months 18–24: beginning to own workstreams and manage upEmbrace the mechanics early — building models quickly and cleanly is genuinely valuable and not "below" you. The analyst who formats perfectly gets noticed; the analyst who rushes formatting to seem more senior is remembered differently
HierarchyClear and enforced. The MD's view takes precedence. You support; you do not lead until explicitly given the responsibility to leadUnderstand that deference is not passivity — you support by being prepared with information, anticipating questions, and making everyone around you more effective

The Behaviours That Distinguish Analysts

WHAT GETS NOTICED POSITIVELY: Ownership: treating every deliverable as if your name is on it Reliability: doing what you say you will do, when you said you would do it Proactivity: flagging issues before being asked, bringing solutions with problems Precision: numbers are right, formatting is consistent, nothing slips through Curiosity: asking intelligent questions that show you are thinking about the deal, not just executing the task WHAT GETS NOTICED NEGATIVELY: Needing to be reminded of open tasks (keep your own task log) Errors that reach senior bankers without being caught internally Not communicating when overloaded (never go quiet — always flag) Treating junior work as beneath you (every analyst formats slides; the best analysts also understand everything in them) Sending emails at unusual hours to signal work rate rather than to actually communicate something necessary
The Most Important First-Year Habit: Managing Up

The single most common failure mode for first-year analysts is not managing upward — not communicating when they are overloaded, when something is unclear, or when a deadline is at risk. MDs and VPs cannot help with a problem they do not know exists. The analyst who says "I have three competing deadlines on Thursday — can you help me prioritise?" is far more effective than the one who silently struggles and misses one. Managing up is not weakness; it is the professional behaviour that allows the team to function under pressure.

In a Real Bank

The analysts who are promoted to associate, recommended for exits, and remembered by MDs a decade later share a common characteristic: they made the people around them better. They caught errors before they reached the client. They flagged process risks early enough that something could be done. They understood the deal well enough to answer a client question when the MD was unavailable. Being technically brilliant is necessary but not sufficient. The analysts who advance are the ones who are reliable, curious, and make the team function at its best.

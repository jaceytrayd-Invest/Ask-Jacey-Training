📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.04 — Debt Schedule & Interest

The debt schedule is one of the most technically demanding components of any financial model. It tracks every debt tranche, calculates interest, models amortisation and sweeps, and handles the circularity with the income statement. Get this right and the rest of the model follows.

Debt Schedule Architecture

Build a separate section for each debt tranche. For each tranche, you need: opening balance, interest rate, interest expense, mandatory amortisation, voluntary repayments (cash sweep), PIK accruals (if applicable), and closing balance.

For each tranche: Opening Balance = Prior year closing balance Interest Rate = hardcoded assumption (SOFR + spread, fixed rate) Interest Expense = Opening balance × rate (or avg balance × rate) Mandatory Amortisation = fixed % of original principal (e.g., 1% TLB) Cash Sweep = Excess FCF × sweep% (see calculation below) PIK Accrual = Opening balance × PIK rate (adds to principal) Closing Balance = Opening − Mandatory Amort − Cash Sweep + PIK Revolver: Opening balance Drawdown / Repayment = plug (draws if cash below minimum; repays if above) Interest = Average drawn balance × RCF rate Closing balance = Opening + Drawdown − Repayment

Cash Sweep Calculation

Step 1: Calculate available FCF Operating FCF = CFO − Capex (from CFS, before debt service) Step 2: Subtract mandatory obligations Available FCF = Operating FCF − Mandatory Amortisation (all tranches) − Cash Interest Step 3: Apply sweep percentage Cash Sweep Amount = MAX(0, Available FCF) × Sweep % (e.g., 75%) MAX(0, ...) ensures no negative sweep if FCF is insufficient Step 4: Apply to debt (usually TLB first, then other tranches) TLB Closing = TLB Opening − Mandatory − Sweep Step 5: Residual cash Residual = Available FCF × (1 − Sweep %) If Residual + Opening Cash > Minimum Cash: repay revolver If Residual + Opening Cash < Minimum Cash: draw revolver

Handling PIK Interest

PIK = Payment In Kind — interest accrues as additional principal IS treatment: PIK interest IS an income statement expense (it appears as interest expense — increases the debt liability) CFS treatment: PIK is a non-cash item — add BACK in CFO (like D&A, no actual cash leaves in the period) Balance Sheet: PIK accrual ADDS to the debt balance each period Closing PIK debt = Opening PIK debt + PIK interest accrued Example: PIK debt: £50m at 15% PIK rate Year 1 PIK accrual: £50m × 15% = £7.5m (added to principal) Year 2 opening PIK balance: £57.5m (£50m + £7.5m) Year 2 PIK accrual: £57.5m × 15% = £8.6m (compounding!)
The Circularity — And How to Resolve It

Interest expense in the IS reduces net income. Lower net income reduces FCF. Less FCF means less cash sweep. Less sweep means higher debt balance. Higher debt balance means more interest. The loop is: interest → FCF → debt → interest.

Resolution 1 (professional): Enable iterative calculation in Excel. Model converges after 100 iterations.
Resolution 2 (simple): Calculate interest on beginning-of-period balance only. No circularity; slight imprecision.
Always confirm which method your firm uses before building a deal model.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

When we review a borrower's model, we rebuild the debt schedule from scratch using our own assumptions. We check: are their interest rates consistent with current market levels? Are they modelling the correct amortisation profile as per the credit agreement? Are cash sweeps applied correctly (borrowers often understate the sweep to show higher cash balances)? Are PIK instruments treated correctly — both the P&L expense and the balance sheet compounding? A debt schedule that doesn't match the credit agreement exactly is a red flag that requires explanation.

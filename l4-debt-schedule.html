📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.04 — Debt & Interest Schedule

The debt schedule is the technical heart of an LBO model. It tracks every tranche of debt, calculates interest, applies mandatory amortisation, executes cash sweeps, and feeds back into the income statement and balance sheet. Get it wrong and every downstream output is wrong.

The Anatomy of a Debt Schedule

For each tranche (TLB, TLA, HY Notes, RCF, Mezzanine): Opening Balance + New drawdowns (at close or during RCF usage) + PIK interest accrued (if applicable) - Mandatory amortisation (fixed % of original principal) - Cash sweep (% of excess FCF after mandatory amort) = Closing Balance Interest Calculation: Cash Interest = Opening Balance × Interest Rate PIK Interest = Opening Balance × PIK Rate (adds to principal) Total Interest = Cash Interest + PIK Interest Commitment Fee (RCF undrawn): = (RCF Limit - RCF Drawn) × Commitment Fee Rate

Order of Operations — critical to get right

The debt repayment waterfall must follow the seniority of claims. Cash is applied in this order:

PriorityPaymentNotes
1Operating costs and taxesBefore any debt service
2Cash interest on RCF (if drawn)RCF is typically super-senior
3Cash interest on TLB (1st lien)Senior secured
4Cash interest on 2nd lien / HY bondsJunior to TLB
5Cash interest on mezzanine (if cash-pay)Most junior debt
6Mandatory TLB amortisationContractually required
7RCF repayment (if drawn, cash sweep to zero first)RCF has first call on excess cash
8TLB cash sweep (50–75% of remaining excess FCF)Optional but contractually specified
9Residual cash retained or distributed to equityLast in the waterfall

PIK Interest — the compounding debt problem

PIK (Payment in Kind) interest is not paid in cash — it is added to the principal balance. This creates compound growth in the debt balance, which creates compound growth in future interest accruals. A £50m mezzanine tranche at 15% PIK (fully PIK) grows to:

PIK debt balance year by year: Y0: £50.0m Y1: £50.0m × (1 + 15%) = £57.5m Y2: £57.5m × (1 + 15%) = £66.1m Y3: £66.1m × (1 + 15%) = £76.0m Y4: £76.0m × (1 + 15%) = £87.5m Y5: £87.5m × (1 + 15%) = £100.6m The original £50m has doubled in 5 years. In the model: PIK accrual each year = Opening Balance × PIK Rate This amount is ADDED to the balance (not paid to the income statement's cash interest line) PIK does NOT reduce FCF — but the compounding burden hits at exit or maturity
The Debt Schedule and the Circularity

The debt schedule creates the model's circularity. Interest expense (from the debt schedule) feeds into the P&L → affects net income → affects CFO → affects how much cash is available for sweeps → affects the closing debt balance → feeds back into next year's interest. Handle this with a circularity switch as described in L4.02 — build the schedule without the switch first, verify it works, then enable the circular reference.

In a Real Bank

In a leveraged finance deal, the debt schedule in the model is reviewed by the bank's credit committee before the bank commits to underwriting. The credit team will run the schedule at base and downside, check that covenants are met, and verify that the mandatory amortisation is achievable from FCF without drawing on the RCF. If the model shows the RCF being drawn in the base case (instead of acting as a contingency), the deal structure is likely too aggressive.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

The first thing we audit in any received model is the debt schedule. We check: (1) is the interest rate correct — are they using the right benchmark (SOFR, not LIBOR)? (2) is the mandatory amortisation rate consistent with the term sheet? (3) is the cash sweep properly computed — are they netting interest correctly before the sweep? (4) does the ending balance reconcile to the balance sheet? Four checks, five minutes. If any fail, we send it back before reading anything else.

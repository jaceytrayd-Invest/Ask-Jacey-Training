📌 Level 03 · Valuation · L3.05 — WACC — Cost of Capital

WACC is the discount rate in a DCF — and therefore one of the two inputs that most moves valuation. Building it properly requires CAPM, beta analysis, and the Hamada relevering equation. Get this right; the DCF follows.

The Formula

WACC = (E/V) × Ke + (D/V) × Kd × (1 - t) Where: E = Market value of equity D = Market value of debt V = E + D (total capital) Ke = Cost of equity (via CAPM) Kd = Pre-tax cost of debt (yield on debt) t = Marginal corporate tax rate

Cost of Equity — CAPM

Ke = Rf + βL × ERP Rf = Risk-free rate (10-year government bond yield) UK: 10Y Gilt yield | US: 10Y Treasury yield βL = Levered beta of the company (vs. market) ERP = Equity Risk Premium (typically 5.0-6.5% for developed markets)

Beta — Unlever and Relever (Hamada)

Beta measures how much the stock moves relative to the market. But in valuation, you should not use the target company's own historical beta — it reflects the wrong capital structure (which changes post-transaction). Instead:

  1. Collect betas for 6–10 comparable companies from Bloomberg
  2. Unlever each peer's beta using Hamada: βU = βL / [1 + (1−t) × D/E]
  3. Take the median unlevered beta from the peer set
  4. Relever to the target's capital structure: βL = βU × [1 + (1−t) × D/E]
Hamada Equations: Unlever: βU = βL / [1 + (1 - t) × (D/E)] Relever: βL = βU × [1 + (1 - t) × (D/E)] Example: Peer levered beta: 1.4, D/E = 0.5, t = 25% βU = 1.4 / [1 + 0.75 × 0.5] = 1.4 / 1.375 = 1.018 Target D/E = 1.0 (60% debt / 40% equity) βL = 1.018 × [1 + 0.75 × 1.0] = 1.018 × 1.75 = 1.781

Cost of Debt

Use the yield on the company's existing debt — the market rate at which they can borrow, not the coupon rate on old bonds. For private companies, estimate from the credit rating implied by their leverage ratios and compare to public debt markets. Then apply the tax shield:

After-tax Kd = Pre-tax yield × (1 - tax rate) Example: 7.0% yield × (1 - 0.25) = 5.25% after-tax cost of debt

Weights — Market Value vs. Book Value

Always use market value weights — not book value. For equity: market cap (shares × price). For debt: market value of debt (which equals face value for investment-grade companies; may differ for distressed). Book value weights produce a WACC that doesn't reflect economic reality.

WACC Sensitivity Check

For most businesses, a 1% change in WACC moves EV by 10–20%. For high-growth businesses with long cash flow tails, the sensitivity is even greater. Before finalising your DCF, stress-test WACC ±1.5% and present the EV impact. This immediately shows the client how sensitive the valuation is to the discount rate assumption.

Common WACC Mistakes

Using book value equity or debt weights. Book value is an accounting construct — market values are what matter economically.

Using the short-term risk-free rate. DCF values long-term cash flows, so use the 10-year government bond — not the 3-month T-bill rate.

Forgetting the tax shield on debt. Interest is tax-deductible. If you use pre-tax cost of debt in WACC without applying (1−t), you are overstating the cost of debt and overstating WACC.

In a Real Bank

WACC is often debated within the deal team. The MD may have a view on the appropriate ERP or risk-free rate. Leverage banks' own published WACC benchmarks by sector where available. In a fairness opinion, the WACC range used (not a single point estimate) must be justifiable to a court — document every input with a source.

📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.02 — Revenue Build & Forecasting

Revenue is the foundation of every financial model. The quality of the entire analysis — valuation, credit, returns — depends on how well the revenue forecast is constructed and how defensible its assumptions are. There is no more important modelling decision than how you build the top line.

Two Fundamentally Different Approaches

Common Revenue Driver Frameworks

Volume × Price (manufacturing, distribution, commodity)

Revenue = Units sold × Average selling price (ASP) Drivers to forecast separately: Volume: capacity utilisation, market demand, new contracts, geographic expansion ASP: pricing power, mix shift (premium vs. standard), inflation pass-through Example: Manufacturer sells 120,000 units at £85 ASP in Year 1 Volume growth: 8%/yr | Price increase: 3%/yr Year 2: 129,600 units × £87.55 = £11.3m Year 3: 139,968 units × £90.18 = £12.6m

Customers × ARPU (SaaS, subscription, telecoms)

Revenue = Customers × ARPU × Months Build customer count as a flow: Opening customers + New customer additions (new logos) − Churned customers (churn rate × opening) = Closing customers Average customers = (Opening + Closing) / 2 ← use for revenue calc Revenue = Average customers × monthly ARPU × 12 Key assumptions: Monthly new logo rate, annual churn %, ARPU, annual price increase %

Contract-Based (professional services, construction, defence)

Revenue = Σ (Contract value × % recognised in period) Steps: 1. List all signed contracts with total value and expected delivery schedule 2. For each contract, apply revenue recognition % per year 3. Add "pipeline" revenue: new contracts expected × win rate × average value × recognition schedule 4. Sum across all contracts to get total revenue Key risk: pipeline win rate assumption — stress test this heavily

Properties × Rent per sq ft (real estate, hospitality, retail chains)

Revenue = Number of properties × Average rent (or RevPAR) × Occupancy For a hotel group: Revenue = Rooms available × Average occupancy % × Average daily rate (ADR) RevPAR = ADR × Occupancy Total Revenue = RevPAR × Available room nights
The Most Important Revenue Modelling Discipline

Every material assumption must be tied to evidence. If you assume 15% revenue growth, you must be able to answer: where is this growth coming from? New customer acquisitions at a specific rate? Geographic expansion? A new product line? "Management told us" is not evidence — management is biased toward optimism. Cross-reference against industry growth rates, historical company performance, and comparable company growth trajectories.

The Revenue Modelling Mistakes That Kill Credibility

"Hockey stick" forecasts with no operational justification. Revenue flat for 3 years then suddenly growing 25% per year — what changed? If you cannot explain the specific operational driver for the inflection point, the model is not credible.

Ignoring churn in subscription models. A subscription business that adds 100 new customers per month but loses 80 through churn is barely growing — not growing at 100/month. Model new additions and churn separately; never model net growth as an assumption without decomposing it.

Applying a constant growth rate without seasonality or cycle adjustment. Many businesses are seasonal or cyclical. Applying a smooth annual growth rate produces unrealistic quarterly patterns that immediately reveal the model as unsophisticated.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We build our own independent revenue model for every credit — we do not accept the borrower's management case. Our approach: take the last three years of actuals, identify the primary revenue drivers, forecast each driver separately under base and downside scenarios, and stress the downside to −25% from our base. Our debt sizing is based on the revenue the business generates in the downside scenario — we lend against downside FCF, not upside potential.

Featured Lesson — The IPO Process

📍 Capital Markets · The IPO Process · L6.02

An IPO (Initial Public Offering) is the most transformative capital markets transaction a company undertakes. It converts a private enterprise into a publicly traded entity — permanently changing its governance, disclosure requirements, and access to capital. Understanding the mechanics from mandate to market is essential for any ECM analyst.

Why Companies Go Public

The decision to IPO is rarely simple. Companies weigh significant benefits against real costs and constraints:

BenefitsCosts & constraints
Access to large-scale permanent equity capitalOngoing disclosure requirements (quarterly/annual filings)
Currency for M&A (listed shares as acquisition consideration)Management time on investor relations, roadshows, earnings calls
Liquidity for founders and early investors (partial exit)Short-term earnings pressure from analyst coverage
Brand and profile enhancement with customers and employeesIPO preparation costs: legal, audit, bank fees (3–7% of proceeds)
Employee equity incentives (options exercisable into listed shares)Loss of control and founder flexibility in decision-making

Valuation in an IPO — different from M&A

IPO valuation uses the same methodologies — trading comps, DCF — but with a critical difference: there is no precedent transaction (the company has not been sold before) and there is no specific acquirer willing to pay a control premium. The valuation is a minority market price, not a control price.

IPO pricing is also market-constrained. Even if the DCF implies £20 per share, if investor appetite only supports £15, the deal prices at £15 — or is pulled. The bookbuild is the market's real-time verdict on valuation.

The Greenshoe Option

The greenshoe (or overallotment option) is a price stabilisation tool. In an IPO, the bookrunners sell 10–15% more shares than the company actually issues — creating a "short position." If the share price falls below the issue price, the stabilisation agent buys shares in the open market (covering the short position), supporting the price. If the price rises, the greenshoe option is exercised — the company issues the overallotment shares at the issue price. The net result: stabilisation without downside risk to the company.

The Two Most Common IPO Failures

Pricing too aggressively. Leaving no money on the table sounds good until the stock drops 15% on day one. Institutional investors who bought at IPO are immediately underwater — they will not participate in the next deal. A well-priced IPO trades up 10–15% on the first day; this "pop" rewards early investors and creates positive momentum. Pricing above this range risks a broken IPO.

Roadshow narrative failing. Investors buy the story before they buy the numbers. A CEO who cannot articulate clearly why the company is going public, what the use of proceeds achieves, and why now is the right moment will lose investor confidence regardless of the financials.

In a Real Bank

ECM analysts during a live IPO work on three simultaneous workstreams: the prospectus (reviewing and updating every financial number in the legal document), the investor materials (the management presentation and its Q&A preparation), and the order book (tracking which investors are building positions and at what price levels). During the roadshow, the analyst is typically running the model updates overnight to incorporate the questions raised in that day's investor meetings — ready for the next morning's session.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

An IPO fundamentally changes a borrower's credit profile — for better and worse. The cash raised strengthens the balance sheet (reducing net leverage). But public company status introduces quarterly earnings pressure, which can lead management to cut investment and optimise for short-term EBITDA rather than long-term business health. Post-IPO, we review our covenant packages — listed companies often negotiate for looser covenants as public disclosure already provides investor accountability. We are generally comfortable with this if the balance sheet has genuinely improved post-listing.

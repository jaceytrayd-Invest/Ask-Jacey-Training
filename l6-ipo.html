📌 Level 06 · Capital Markets · L6.02 — The IPO Process

The IPO is the most consequential capital markets transaction a company undertakes. It permanently changes the company's governance, disclosure obligations, and investor base. Understanding every stage — and the specific role of the investment bank at each — is essential for any ECM professional.

The Six-Stage IPO Timeline

StageDurationKey deliverables
1. Preparation3–12 months3-year audited accounts; corporate governance restructuring; advisor appointments; prospectus drafting begins; pre-deal investor education (PDIE)
2. Pilot fishing2–4 weeksConfidential investor meetings (10-20 key institutions); test equity story and valuation range; no commitment made
3. Intention to Float (ITF)1 dayPublic announcement; pathfinder prospectus filed; analyst research published (banks' research separated from corporate finance by wall)
4. Roadshow10–14 days50–100+ investor meetings; London, Edinburgh, New York, Paris, Frankfurt; CEO/CFO led; order book builds
5. Bookbuild & Pricing2–5 daysFormal orders collected; price discovery; allocation; final prospectus filed with price
6. Admission & Stabilisation30 days post-listingShares admitted to trading; greenshoe stabilisation; lock-up enforcement; analyst coverage initiation

IPO Valuation — minority pricing, not control pricing

IPO valuation differs from M&A valuation in one critical respect: there is no control premium. An IPO prices at a minority market valuation — what a diversified institutional investor will pay for a liquid, exchange-traded minority stake. This is consistently lower than what an acquirer would pay for 100% of the business (which includes synergies and control premium).

IPO methodologies: trading comparables (primary) and DCF (cross-check). No precedent transactions — there is no comparable transaction for a first-time listing. The peer group selection is paramount — bankers must find listed companies whose businesses are genuinely comparable in growth profile and margin structure.

The Prospectus — the IPO's legal backbone

The prospectus is the legal disclosure document filed with the regulator (FCA in the UK). It must contain all material information for investors to make an informed decision — the company's business, financials (3 years), risk factors, use of proceeds, and selling shareholders. The pathfinder prospectus (filed at ITF) omits the final price and allocation; the final prospectus adds these post-pricing.

Prospectus structure (UK Listing Rules / Prospectus Regulation): 1. Summary (brief overview for retail investors) 2. Risk factors (legal, operational, financial, regulatory, market) 3. Business description (products, markets, competitive position) 4. Operating and financial review (management discussion of financials) 5. Directors and senior management 6. Financial statements (3 years, IFRS, audited) 7. Use of proceeds 8. Selling shareholders 9. Lock-up arrangements 10. Details of the offer (structure, timetable, allocation policy)
The IPO "Window" — Market Timing

IPOs are acutely sensitive to market conditions. An IPO launched into a falling market typically fails or is pulled — institutional investors will not commit capital to a new, illiquid position when their existing portfolios are declining. The "IPO window" refers to periods of stable or rising equity markets, low volatility (VIX), and strong appetite for new issuances. The 2021 IPO wave (post-Covid stimulus) and the 2022 drought (rising rates, market falls) perfectly illustrate how quickly the window opens and closes. ECM bankers monitor market sentiment daily and advise clients on timing accordingly.

In a Real Bank

The analyst's role in an IPO is intensely multi-threaded. During preparation: building and maintaining the financial model, drafting the financial sections of the CIM/prospectus, running the comps analysis, and responding to legal counsel's financial queries. During roadshow: updating the model overnight with investor Q&A from the day's meetings, preparing daily order book summaries, and supporting the roadshow logistics. During pricing: running sensitivity analysis on allocation scenarios and pricing levels. Post-IPO: producing the aftermarket performance report and monitoring the stabilisation window.

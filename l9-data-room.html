📌 Level 09 · Deal Execution · L9.04 — Data Rooms — Setup & Management

The virtual data room (VDR) is the operational infrastructure of every M&A, ECM, and leveraged finance transaction. Its quality signals the quality of the deal team. A well-structured, complete, and carefully permissioned VDR accelerates due diligence and reduces buyer queries. A disorganised one creates delays, raises red flags, and generates unnecessary price chips.

VDR Structure — the standard index

Standard M&A sell-side VDR index: 01. Corporate 01.1 Corporate structure chart and group diagram 01.2 Certificate of incorporation and constitutional documents 01.3 Shareholder register and cap table 01.4 Board composition and director details 01.5 Significant subsidiary information 02. Financial 02.1 Audited accounts — last 3 financial years 02.2 Management accounts — last 24 months (monthly) 02.3 Budget and reforecast — current year 02.4 Financial model (management's) 02.5 Working capital analysis 03. Legal 03.1 Key customer contracts (top 10 by revenue) 03.2 Key supplier and vendor agreements 03.3 Lease agreements and property documents 03.4 IP register — patents, trademarks, software titles 03.5 Litigation and disputes register 03.6 Regulatory licences and permits 04. Commercial (second round only — restricted access) 04.1 Customer details — top 10 by revenue with contract terms 04.2 Sales pipeline and CRM data 04.3 Pricing schedules 05. HR 05.1 Headcount by function and grade (anonymised) 05.2 Org chart (to C-suite minus one) 05.3 Senior management compensation (CEO, CFO, COO) 05.4 Pension and benefit plan summaries 06. Tax 06.1 Tax returns — last 3 years 06.2 Correspondence with HMRC 06.3 Transfer pricing documentation 07. Operations and IT 07.1 Key operational procedures 07.2 IT system architecture overview 07.3 Cybersecurity posture summary

Access Permissions — the critical control mechanism

The most operationally sensitive aspect of VDR management is access permissions. Each buyer group must be given access only to the documents appropriate for their stage of the process — and critically, no buyer can see another buyer's activity.

Permission structure by process stage: First-round buyers (IOI stage): Access: Sections 01, 02, 03.1 (anonymised), 05 (headcount summary) Restricted: 04 (commercial), 06 (tax), detailed customer contracts Second-round buyers (DD stage): Access: Full VDR access (all sections) Exception: Section 04 (customer detail) may still require separate approval Preferred bidder (exclusivity): Access: All sections + management reference calls May access board minutes (with legal redaction) Critical rules: → Set permissions at the FOLDER level, not document level (document-level permissions are error-prone) → Verify permissions with a test login before any buyer is given access → Check access logs weekly — know which documents each buyer has seen → When uploading new documents: check permission before uploading
The Q&A Log — Keeping the Process Fair and Defensible

Every question submitted by any buyer is logged in a master Q&A log. When the same question arrives from multiple buyers, the same answer is provided to all of them — simultaneously. This consistency is both a legal requirement (information must be provided equally to all bidders) and a practical necessity (inconsistent answers become evidence in post-closing disputes). The analyst owns the Q&A log: routing questions to the right management contact, reviewing draft answers before release, tracking response status, and ensuring no question goes unanswered for more than 48 hours without a status update to the buyer.

In a Real Bank

Analysts spend a disproportionate amount of their time on live deal VDR management — it is unglamorous but operationally critical. The most common analyst errors: uploading a document without checking permissions (so the wrong buyer sees it), deleting a document instead of archiving it (breaking the audit trail), and failing to track Q&A response timelines (buyers escalate to MD level when queries go unanswered for days). All three are avoidable with checklists and discipline. Senior bankers remember which analysts they can trust to manage a VDR cleanly.

📌 Level 08 · Private Markets · L8.06 — PE Exit Strategies & Secondaries

The exit is where PE returns are crystallised. Choosing the right exit route — and timing it correctly — can be the difference between a 2.5x and a 3.5x return on the same investment. This lesson covers the four primary exit paths, the emerging secondary market, and how timing affects IRR.

The Four PE Exit Routes

RouteDescriptionTypical multiple achievedBest conditions
Trade saleSell to a strategic corporate acquirerHighest — includes synergy premium (control premium 20-40% vs. standalone)Active strategic buyer universe; business has clear strategic value to acquirers
IPOList shares on a public exchange; PE firm sells stake over time (lock-up then block trades)High — especially for high-growth businesses where public markets re-rate to premium multiplesStrong equity markets; growth/quality company with compelling public narrative
Secondary buyout (SBO)Sell to another PE firmLower than trade sale — financial buyer only, no synergiesNo strategic buyer available; business has remaining value creation runway for a new sponsor
Dividend recapitalisationBorrow at portfolio company level; pay dividend to PE firm without sellingPartial return — improves IRR without full exitCompany has significant debt capacity; strong FCF generation; M&A market conditions unfavourable for full exit

The Timing Effect on IRR

Same £100m equity cheque, same £250m exit proceeds (2.5x MOIC): Exit in Year 3: IRR = 2.5^(1/3) - 1 = 35.7% Exit in Year 5: IRR = 2.5^(1/5) - 1 = 20.1% Exit in Year 7: IRR = 2.5^(1/7) - 1 = 14.0% Exit in Year 9: IRR = 2.5^(1/9) - 1 = 10.7% The IRR impact of holding 2 years longer: Year 3 to Year 5: -15.6 percentage points Year 5 to Year 7: -6.1 percentage points Year 7 to Year 9: -3.3 percentage points The early years have the most IRR leverage — holding one more year from Year 3 to Year 4 costs more IRR than holding from Year 8 to Year 9. Implication: PE firms are strongly incentivised to exit early-performing deals quickly (to maximise IRR) and are forced to hold underperforming deals (because the exit multiple would be too low to show an acceptable return).

The Secondary Market — GP-led transactions

The secondary market allows LP investors to sell their PE fund stakes before the fund matures — providing liquidity in an otherwise illiquid asset class. The two types:

LP-led secondaries: An LP sells its stake in a fund to a secondary buyer (e.g., Lexington Partners, Ardian, HarbourVest). The buyer acquires the LP's rights to future distributions at a discount to NAV. The GP is informed but typically not involved.

GP-led secondaries (continuation funds): The GP moves one or more "trophy assets" from a maturing fund into a new vehicle, giving existing LPs the choice to cash out or roll into the continuation vehicle. New capital comes in alongside rolling LPs. Allows the GP to hold high-conviction assets beyond the fund's normal 10-year term. Has grown significantly — approximately 40-50% of secondary market volume by 2023.

The Exit Dual-Track

A sophisticated PE sponsor will often run a dual-track exit process simultaneously — preparing for both an IPO and a trade sale at the same time. The IPO preparation forces the company to achieve public-company readiness (audited financials, governance, investor communications) which itself makes the company more attractive to trade buyers. The two processes compete — if a strategic buyer emerges with a compelling price, the sponsor takes the trade sale. If strategic interest is limited, the IPO provides an alternative. The dual-track is expensive and management-intensive but consistently produces better outcomes than choosing a single path.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

When a portfolio company is sold (the PE firm exits), our loan must typically be repaid in full at closing — most private credit loans include a change-of-control provision requiring prepayment. We receive par plus any applicable prepayment premium (typically a call premium in early years — e.g., 103% in Year 1, 102% in Year 2, 101% in Year 3, then par). The prepayment premium compensates us for losing an attractive yield-bearing asset. If the new owner wants to continue with our financing, we negotiate a new facility from scratch — resetting terms for the new sponsor and updated business profile.

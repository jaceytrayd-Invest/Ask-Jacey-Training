The Income Statement

The income statement answers one question: how profitable was this business over a given period? It is the statement most often quoted in media, most often manipulated by management, and most often misread by beginners. Understanding every line — and its limitations — is non-negotiable.

The P&L Architecture

The income statement flows top-down from revenue to net income. Each major subtotal has a name and a purpose. Learn them in order.

Line ItemDefinitionIB Significance
RevenueTotal income from core business activity — sales of goods, services, or both. Also called "turnover" in UK/European contexts.Top-line growth rate is the first filter in any business analysis. Organic vs. acquired growth is key.
Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)Direct costs of producing revenue — raw materials, direct labour, manufacturing overhead. Excludes SG&A and D&A.COGS as % of revenue measures production efficiency. Rising COGS margin = pricing or cost pressure.
Gross ProfitRevenue − COGS. The first profitability gate.Gross margin reveals pricing power. Software: 70–80%. Manufacturing: 20–40%. Retail: 25–45%.
SG&ASelling, General & Administrative — salaries, marketing, rent, back-office costs not tied directly to production.Operating leverage lives here. Fixed SG&A + growing revenue = expanding margins.
R&DResearch and development spend. Either expensed (hits P&L) or capitalised (balance sheet intangible).Capitalisation policy is a key QoE question. Tech and pharma companies are most affected.
EBITDAEarnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation. Gross Profit − SG&A − R&D (before D&A).The primary valuation multiple denominator. Used in EV/EBITDA. Approximates operating cash generation.
D&ADepreciation & Amortisation — non-cash charge allocating historical asset cost over its useful life.Added back in CFS. Affects EBIT but not EBITDA. Key in capex-heavy businesses.
EBITEarnings Before Interest & Tax. EBITDA − D&A. Also called "Operating Profit."Capital-structure neutral. Use EV/EBIT for businesses with widely varying D&A profiles.
Interest ExpenseCost of debt. Reduces pre-tax profit. Creates the tax shield.Interest cover (EBITDA/interest) is the primary credit covenant metric.
EBTEarnings Before Tax. EBIT − Net Interest.Rarely used as a standalone metric in IB. Useful for tax analysis.
TaxCurrent and deferred income tax charge. Rate varies by jurisdiction and structure.Effective tax rate ≠ statutory tax rate. Deferred taxes, tax credits, and losses affect the actual rate.
Net IncomeThe "bottom line." Profit attributable to shareholders after all charges. Feeds retained earnings on the BS.Basis for EPS and P/E ratios. Less useful than EBITDA for cross-company comparison due to leverage effects.

Key Margins and what they reveal

Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue → Pricing power, production efficiency EBITDA Margin = EBITDA / Revenue → Operating cash generation before capex EBIT Margin = EBIT / Revenue → Profitability after non-cash asset charges Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue → Bottom-line after debt cost and tax
The Margin Ladder

Each margin is lower than the one above it. The gap between gross margin and EBITDA margin shows the operating cost burden (SG&A, R&D). The gap between EBITDA and EBIT shows D&A intensity (capex-heavy businesses show a large gap). The gap between EBIT and net income shows the debt burden (highly leveraged companies show a large gap). Each gap tells a story about cost structure.

Operating Leverage — the hidden multiplier

A business with high fixed costs and low variable costs has high operating leverage. When revenue grows, a large portion flows through to EBITDA because the fixed cost base barely moves. When revenue falls, the same fixed costs crush margins.

High operating leverage businesses (airlines, hotels, software, media) have EBITDA margins that swing dramatically with revenue changes. Low operating leverage businesses (distributors, staffing agencies) have thin but stable margins. In a deal context, you must understand which type you are buying — the valuation multiple and the downside risk both follow from this.

Common Mistakes

Confusing EBITDA with cash flow. EBITDA ignores capex, working capital movements, and cash taxes. A business with £100m EBITDA and £90m capex is not "highly profitable" from a cash perspective. Always bridge to FCF.

Comparing P/E ratios across leveraged businesses. Net income is after interest — so a highly leveraged company has depressed earnings and a high P/E even if its underlying business is not expensive. Use EV/EBITDA for cross-capital-structure comparisons.

In a Real Bank

When analysts "spread" comps — building a comparable company table — they pull 5–7 years of income statement data for each peer. They normalise for one-off items, standardise definitions of EBITDA, and present LTM (Last Twelve Months) figures alongside NTM (Next Twelve Months) consensus estimates. The spread takes a junior analyst 2–3 days to build properly for a 10-company peer set.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

From a lending standpoint, the income statement tells us about the business's earnings capacity. But we never rely on it alone. We normalise EBITDA (removing one-off items), stress-test it downward, and then compare it to debt service requirements. Our primary covenant is typically Net Leverage (Net Debt / EBITDA) — requiring that the EBITDA figure in this ratio is adjusted, audited, and tested against a trailing twelve-month period, not a forward projection.

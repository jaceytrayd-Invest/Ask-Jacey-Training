📌 Level 10 · Careers · L10.04 — The IB Hierarchy — Roles and Progression

The IB career ladder is well-defined, globally consistent, and relatively transparent in its progression criteria. Understanding what each level does — and what it takes to advance — is the foundation of intentional career management rather than hoping the right opportunity appears.

The Five-Level IB Career Ladder

Analyst2–3 yearsBuilding models, pitch books, managing data rooms, running process logistics. Learning the craft of financial analysis under supervision.Technical excellence, reliability, institutional knowledge. Promote-to-associate or external exit.

Vice President (VP)3–5 yearsRunning transactions semi-independently, managing the team on live deals, deepening client relationships, beginning to originate smaller mandates. The bridge between execution and origination.Demonstrated ability to win and manage client relationships independently. Revenue generation beginning to matter.

Compensation by Level — illustrative London ranges

Analyst (years 1-3): £80,000 – £150,000 all-in Associate (years 1-3): £130,000 – £250,000 all-in VP (years 1-3): £200,000 – £400,000 all-in Director: £300,000 – £600,000 all-in MD: £500,000 – £3,000,000+ all-in Note: these ranges are illustrative and vary significantly by: Bank (bulge bracket vs. boutique vs. regional) Product group (M&A typically higher than ECM) Performance (bonus is a variable multiplier — top performers earn 2–3× the low end of each range) Year (compensation resets annually based on bank and group performance) Market cycle (2021 was exceptional; 2023 was significantly lower) The bonus dominates at every level above analyst: At MD level, bonus is typically 2–5× base salary A strong MD at a top bank in a good year earns £1–3m total
The Hardest Transition: VP to Director

Most analysts and associates who stay in banking make it to VP. The VP-to-Director transition is where many experienced bankers plateau — because it requires a fundamental shift from execution (doing the work) to origination (bringing in the work). Execution skill is measurable and improvable. Origination ability is less teachable — it depends on the depth of client trust built over years, the quality of the banker's judgment, and whether clients think of this person when they have a problem. MDs who consistently generate £5–10m+ of revenue per year are rare and extremely well compensated. The ones who cannot make that transition often leave for corporate roles at VP or Director level.

In a Real Bank

The promotion process is neither entirely meritocratic nor entirely political — it is both. Strong execution record is necessary but not sufficient. Being seen by the right MDs, being staffed on high-profile deals, and having an internal advocate who believes in you and mentions your name in promotion discussions — all of these matter as much as the objective quality of your work. This is why developing genuine relationships with senior bankers who know your work — not just those you are directly staffed under — is a core career management activity.

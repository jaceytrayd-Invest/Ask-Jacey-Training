Featured Lesson — Accretion / Dilution Analysis

📍 M&A · Accretion / Dilution · L5.03

Accretion/dilution analysis answers the most immediate question a corporate acquirer asks about any deal: does buying this company increase or decrease our earnings per share? If it increases EPS, the deal is accretive. If it decreases EPS, it is dilutive. Understanding why — and what drives the outcome — is one of the most tested M&A concepts in IB interviews.

The Core Logic

When a company acquires another, it gains the target's earnings but also incurs a financing cost — either interest expense (on acquisition debt) or equity dilution (from new shares issued). The deal is accretive if the earnings gained exceed the financing cost. Dilutive if they don't.

Pro Forma EPS = (Acquirer Net Income + Target Net Income + Synergies − Financing Cost) ÷ (Acquirer Shares Outstanding + New Shares Issued) Accretion / (Dilution) % = (Pro Forma EPS − Standalone EPS) / Standalone EPS If positive → Accretive (EPS increased) If negative → Dilutive (EPS decreased)

Cash Deal — when is it accretive?

In a cash deal, the acquirer borrows money (or uses existing cash) to buy the target. The financing cost is the after-tax interest on the acquisition debt.

Cash Deal Accretion Rule: After-tax return on acquisition > After-tax cost of debt After-tax return = Target Net Income / Purchase Price After-tax cost of debt = Interest Rate × (1 − Tax Rate) Example: Purchase price: £200m Target net income: £14m After-tax return: £14m / £200m = 7.0% Debt at 6%, tax rate 25%: After-tax cost of debt: 6.0% × 0.75 = 4.5% 7.0% > 4.5% → Cash deal is ACCRETIVE

Stock Deal — when is it accretive?

In a stock deal, the acquirer issues new shares. The "financing cost" is the dilution of existing shareholders — new shares absorb some of the target's earnings without adding incremental income.

Stock Deal Accretion Rule: Acquirer P/E > Target P/E (on a purchase price basis) Acquirer P/E = Acquirer Share Price / Acquirer EPS Target P/E = Purchase Price per Share / Target EPS If Acquirer P/E > Target P/E → Stock deal ACCRETIVE If Acquirer P/E < Target P/E → Stock deal DILUTIVE Intuition: If you are a high-P/E company buying a low-P/E company using stock, each new share you issue "brings in" more earnings than the share you gave away was "worth" — net accretive.

Mixed Consideration — cash and stock

Most large M&A deals use a mix of cash and stock. The accretion/dilution calculation blends both components: partial cash financing (with its interest cost) and partial stock issuance (with its dilution effect). The optimal mix depends on the acquirer's current P/E, the cost of debt, and management's preference for maintaining balance sheet flexibility.

The Critical Nuance — Accretive Does Not Mean Value-Creating

A deal can be EPS-accretive and still destroy shareholder value. If the acquirer overpays by £100m but the deal is accretive (because the target's earnings exceed the financing cost), the acquirer's equity is worth less — they paid too much. Accretion/dilution tells you about the short-term EPS impact. It does not assess whether the purchase price was fair or whether the deal creates long-term value. Both analyses are needed — accretion/dilution AND a full DCF / IRR on the acquisition.

The Three Most Common Acc/Dil Mistakes

1. Forgetting the amortisation of intangibles. In a stock deal especially, PPA (purchase price allocation) creates intangible assets that amortise — reducing the target's contribution to pro forma earnings. Including only pre-PPA earnings overstates accretion.

2. Using gross interest rather than after-tax. Interest on acquisition debt is tax-deductible. The actual cost to the acquirer is the pre-tax rate × (1 − tax rate). Using the gross rate understates accretion or overstates dilution in a cash deal.

3. Ignoring integration costs. Restructuring charges, IT system migration costs, redundancy payments — these reduce pro forma earnings in Years 1–2. A deal that looks accretive in Year 1 (before integration costs) may be dilutive in Year 2 when those costs hit.

In a Real Bank

The accretion/dilution model is typically one tab in the larger M&A model. It sits alongside the DCF and comps analysis. In a strategic advisory engagement, the banker presents three analyses to the acquirer's board: "The deal is accretive to EPS by 4% in Year 1. The implied IRR on the acquisition is 18%, above our WACC of 11%. Our DCF analysis suggests we are paying a 22% premium to intrinsic value — within the range of historical control premiums for this sector." The board needs all three lenses to make a properly informed decision.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We are largely indifferent to whether an acquisition is EPS-accretive for the buyer — that is an equity investor metric. Our concern is: does the combined entity generate sufficient FCF to service the acquisition debt? If a strategic acquirer borrows £500m at 7% to fund an acquisition, we assess whether the combined EBITDA (net of integration costs and realistic synergies) provides adequate interest coverage and deleveraging capacity. Accretion tells us nothing about those questions — only the FCF and leverage profile does.

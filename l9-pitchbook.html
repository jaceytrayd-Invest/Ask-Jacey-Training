📌 Level 09 · Deal Execution · L9.01 — The Pitch Book — Architecture & Craft

The pitch book is the primary deliverable of the IB analyst and the document that wins mandates. It is built under time pressure, reviewed by multiple layers of senior bankers, and ultimately presented to a client board. Building one well — with accuracy, narrative coherence, and design discipline — is the first craft every analyst must master.

The Six Sections and What Each Must Achieve

SectionWhat it must achieveCommon failure modes
1. Situation OverviewDemonstrate the bank understands the company deeply — not just what is on the website, but the nuances of the competitive position, the recent performance trend, and why this moment mattersGeneric description copied from the annual report; no differentiated insight
2. Strategic ContextEstablish the "why now" case — M&A activity in the sector, the macro window, the competitive dynamics that make a transaction compelling at this point in the cycleHistorical narrative with no forward-looking view; fails to connect sector trends to the specific company
3. Valuation — Football FieldAnchor price expectations with a defensible, multi-methodology range. Every bar on the football field must be independently supportable under senior interrogationComp set selected to justify a predetermined conclusion; DCF assumptions not stress-tested; range too narrow to be credible
4. Buyer UniverseDemonstrate the bank has relationships with the buyers who matter; show why each would be interested; reveal the depth of sell-side market knowledgeGeneric list of large companies in the sector without any analysis of rationale, capacity, or likelihood
5. Process RecommendationShow the bank has thought through how the process should be run — not just that they want the mandateBoilerplate "we recommend a structured auction" without explaining why this specific structure fits this specific situation
6. CredentialsProve the bank has done this before at this size in this sector — and done it wellCredentials from five years ago or from adjacent sectors that do not directly answer "have you sold a business like ours?"

The Football Field — built correctly

For a sell-side pitch, the football field shows IMPLIED EQUITY VALUE ranges: Trading Comps: Identify 6-10 listed peers → calculate their EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E Apply 25th-75th percentile range to target's metrics Bridge from EV to equity: subtract net debt Precedent Transactions: Identify 8-15 M&A deals in the sector (last 5-7 years) Extract EV/EBITDA and EV/Revenue acquisition multiples Apply 25th-75th percentile to target Note: precedents typically show a control premium vs. trading comps DCF: Build model with 5-year explicit forecast Terminal value via Gordon Growth (g = 2.0-3.0%) or exit multiple Sensitise: WACC ±1%, terminal growth ±0.5% Low end = high WACC, low growth | High end = low WACC, high growth 52-Week Range (listed targets only): Actual observed market price × diluted share count Adjusted for net debt to get EV range Reference Lines: Current/undisturbed share price → where the market currently values it Proposed offer price → what the buyer is actually offering (Offer price should fall at or above the mid-point of the overlap zone)

Craft Principles — what MDs look for in review

Slide titles are conclusions, not labels. "Revenue growth has accelerated to 18% over three years" is a title. "Revenue Growth 2021–2024" is a label. The reader should understand the point before looking at the chart — not after.

One idea per slide. If a slide takes more than 15 seconds to read and understand, it has too much on it. Everything else goes in the appendix. MDs presenting in a client meeting cannot explain a complex slide while simultaneously watching the client's face for reaction — simplicity enables presentation fluency.

Every number has a source cell. When the MD is challenged on a valuation metric in the client meeting, they turn to the analyst for confirmation. The analyst must be able to say "slide 14, comp table, third row" — not "I think I remember." Every data point in the pitch book traces back to a specific cell in the model or a named market data source.

The Review Cycle

A pitch book typically goes through five rounds of review over 48–72 hours. The analyst builds the first draft. The associate or VP reviews for factual accuracy and adds analytical depth. The Director reviews for narrative and strategic framing. The MD reviews for client fit — does this book make the right argument for this client in this situation? Final check: a second analyst reviews every number against source data. Each review round arrives at night; the analyst incorporates changes overnight and delivers the next version by 8am.

In a Real Bank

The pitch book template is sacred. Every bank maintains a proprietary template — standardised slide layouts, approved fonts, colour palette, formatting conventions. Deviating from the template requires explicit MD approval. This is brand management: Goldman's materials look like Goldman's. The visual consistency is a credibility signal built over decades. Analysts who produce consistently clean, on-template materials get noticed — the quality of your pitch book is visible evidence of the quality of your work to everyone in the building.

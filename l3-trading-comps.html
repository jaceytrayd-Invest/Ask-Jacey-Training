📌 Level 03 · Valuation · L3.02 — Trading Comparables

Trading comparables is the valuation methodology most used in day-to-day IB work. It is market-based, transparent, and anchored in observable prices. Master the process and the judgement calls — they are what separate adequate analysts from excellent ones.

Step 1 — Define the peer universe

Peer selection is the most important — and most judgemental — step. Use these criteria in order of importance:

CriterionWhy it mattersHow to apply
Business modelRevenue model, customer type, value chain position must be similarRead each company's 10-K/annual report business description
Sector/sub-sectorIndustry dynamics drive multiples more than any other factorUse GICS or SIC codes as a starting screen; refine manually
GeographyRegional market dynamics affect margins and growthPrefer same-region peers; flag if mixing geographies
SizeLarge-caps trade at premiums to small-caps (liquidity, scale)Screen within ±3× revenue; note if forced to use different sizes
Margin profileBusinesses with 60% EBITDA margins should not be in the same set as 15% margin businessesCheck EBITDA margin for each peer; flag outliers

Step 2 — Spread the financials

For each peer, collect LTM and NTM financials: Revenue, Gross Profit, EBITDA, EBIT, Net Income, EPS. Source from Bloomberg or CapIQ — but cross-check every figure against the actual filing. CapIQ contains errors, particularly for operating lease adjustments under IFRS 16.

Normalise for non-recurring items. If a peer had a large one-off in their LTM period, adjust it out so the comparison is apples-to-apples. Document every adjustment.

Step 3 — Calculate EV for each peer

EV = Market Cap + Net Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred Equity = (Shares × Price) + (Gross Debt − Cash) + Minority Interest + Preferred Key data sources: Market cap: Bloomberg, real-time Shares: diluted (includes in-the-money options, convertibles) Debt: latest balance sheet (may need IFRS 16 lease adjustment) Cash: latest balance sheet — exclude restricted cash

Step 4 — Calculate and present multiples

MultipleFormulaWhen to useTypical range
EV/EBITDAEV ÷ LTM EBITDAUniversal — primary multiple for most sectors6–20x depending on sector
EV/EBITEV ÷ LTM EBITWhen D&A differences are meaningful across peers8–25x
EV/RevenueEV ÷ LTM RevenuePre-profit businesses; SaaS; high-growth tech1–15x
P/EPrice ÷ LTM EPSEquity-level comparison; mature businesses; financials10–35x
NTM EV/EBITDAEV ÷ NTM EBITDA consensusShow growth trajectory; relevant when LTM is distortedSimilar to LTM but lower if growth expected

Step 5 — Derive implied valuation

Implied EV = Subject EBITDA × Median peer EV/EBITDA Implied Equity Value = Implied EV − Subject Net Debt − Minority − Preferred Implied Share Price = Implied Equity Value ÷ Diluted Shares Outstanding

Step 6 — Sense check and present range

Apply both median and 25th–75th percentile range. The median anchors your recommendation; the range shows upside and downside. Always compare the implied range to the current trading price and explain any premium or discount.

The Three Mistakes That Will Get You Caught

1. Using gross debt instead of net debt. Every time. Net debt = gross debt minus cash. Check the cash balance.

2. Using basic shares instead of diluted. Diluted includes options and convertibles in-the-money. CapIQ has a diluted share count — use it.

3. Not normalising peers for one-off items. If a peer had a large restructuring in the LTM period, their EBITDA is understated. Your multiple for that peer will be artificially high and skew your median. Adjust it out and note the adjustment.

In a Real Bank

Building a comps table for a mid-sized deal takes a junior analyst 1–2 full days. Data collection, checking against filings, normalisation, formatting, and cross-checking consume the bulk of the time. The MD will likely ask you to re-run with a different peer set or different normalisation at least once. Every figure must be defensible in a client meeting — you should be able to say "that number comes from page 34 of their last annual report" for any figure in your table.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We use trading comps to establish trough valuation — the floor value we could recover in a distressed sale. We apply the lowest credible peer multiple (25th percentile or trough cycle multiple) to the borrower's EBITDA and compare it to our loan balance. If this trough EV comfortably exceeds our debt, we have collateral coverage. If not, we either require additional security, a lower loan amount, or we decline to lend.

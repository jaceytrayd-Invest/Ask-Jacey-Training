📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.05 — Modelling Working Capital & Capex

Working capital and capex are the two components of FCF most commonly modelled incorrectly by junior analysts. Getting them right separates a "good enough" model from a professional one — and directly affects the debt capacity and returns calculation.

Working Capital — the days-based approach

Input assumptions (all blue, in Inputs tab): DSO: Days Sales Outstanding (e.g., 45 days) DIO: Days Inventory Outstanding (e.g., 30 days) DPO: Days Payable Outstanding (e.g., 55 days) Balance sheet calculations: Accounts Receivable = (Revenue / 365) × DSO Inventory = (COGS / 365) × DIO Accounts Payable = (COGS / 365) × DPO Year-on-year changes flow to CFS: ΔNWC = ΔAP - ΔAR - ΔInventory (positive ΔNWC = source of cash; negative = use of cash) Note: ΔNWC is SUBTRACTED from FCF in the CFS: FCF = CFO - Capex CFO = Net Income + D&A - ΔNWC (increase in WC = use of cash = reduces FCF)

Common Working Capital Modelling Errors

ErrorWhat happensCorrect approach
Using the same COGS figure for both AR and APAR should use Revenue; AP should use COGS. Mixing them creates wrong balances.AR = (Revenue / 365) × DSO. AP = (COGS / 365) × DPO.
Not modelling deferred revenueSaaS and subscription businesses collect cash before recognising revenue. Ignoring this understates cash and overstates WC.Deferred revenue = Prepaid customer cash not yet earned. Rising deferred revenue = source of cash (model like AP — a liability).
Assuming flat WC as % of revenueMisses the cash impact of changing payment terms, growing AR books, inventory build in expansion.Model each WC line independently via days. Let the model calculate the WC balance dynamically.
Forgetting the Year 0 → Year 1 WC changeThe first year of the forecast period must compare against the last year of history — not against zero. Missing this creates a phantom WC movement in Year 1.Always include the prior year (Year 0 / LTM) WC balance as the starting point in the WC schedule.

Capex — the PP&E roll

PP&E Roll (Gross): Opening Gross PP&E + Capex additions - Disposals (at gross cost) = Closing Gross PP&E Accumulated Depreciation Roll: Opening Accumulated D&A + D&A charge for the year ← feeds to P&L as an expense - Accumulated D&A on disposals = Closing Accumulated D&A Net PP&E = Closing Gross PP&E - Closing Accumulated D&A D&A Calculation (straight-line, simplified): D&A = Opening Gross PP&E / Useful Life New capex D&A = Capex / Useful Life (pro-rated in year of acquisition) More precise: build a waterfall of each vintage of capex with its own useful life and depreciation schedule

Capex as a Forecast Driver

Capex is typically modelled as a percentage of revenue (e.g., 8% of revenue per year) or as a fixed management-guided amount. Split into maintenance (≈D&A) and growth (balance). In a downside scenario, growth capex is the first item to cut — model this explicitly with a scenario toggle that reduces capex to maintenance-only under stress.

The Cash Conversion Cycle in Your Model

The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC = DSO + DIO − DPO) tells you how many days elapse between spending cash (on inventory, labour) and receiving cash (from customers). A falling CCC means the business is converting to cash faster — beneficial for FCF. A rising CCC means cash is being consumed by the working capital cycle. Model the CCC trend explicitly: if a fast-growing company has a rising CCC (common — growth requires more WC investment), this is a significant FCF drag that many analysts miss.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We separately model maintenance capex (unavoidable) and growth capex (discretionary). In a distressed scenario, we assume growth capex falls to zero — the company cuts all expansion investment to preserve cash for debt service. If even maintenance-only capex (equal to D&A) plus mandatory debt service exceeds CFO, the company cannot sustain its asset base AND service its debt simultaneously. This is the threshold at which we are looking at a restructuring rather than a covenant waiver.

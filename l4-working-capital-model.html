📌 Level 04 · Modelling · L4.05 — Modelling Working Capital

Working capital modelling is where many financial models are weakest. Getting the days-based mechanics right, and understanding how WC changes flow through the cash flow statement, is the difference between a model that correctly projects cash and one that systematically misrepresents liquidity.

The Days-Based Framework

Accounts Receivable: AR = Revenue × DSO / 365 DSO = Days Sales Outstanding (how many days to collect from customers) Inventory: Inventory = COGS × DIO / 365 DIO = Days Inventory Outstanding (how many days to sell stock) Accounts Payable: AP = COGS × DPO / 365 DPO = Days Payable Outstanding (how many days to pay suppliers) Why these drivers? AR is driven by Revenue → customers owe you for sales made Inventory is driven by COGS → stock is valued at cost AP is driven by COGS → you owe suppliers for stock purchased DO NOT use Revenue as the driver for AP — this is a common error. AP should reference COGS, not Revenue.

WC Changes in the Cash Flow Statement

CFS Working Capital Adjustments: − Increase in AR = −(AR_end − AR_start) − Increase in Inventory = −(Inv_end − Inv_start) + Increase in AP = +(AP_end − AP_start) + Increase in Accruals = +(Accr_end − Accr_start) Shorthand: Current Operating Asset increases → subtract from CFO (use of cash) Current Operating Liability increases → add to CFO (source of cash) Excel formula example for AR adjustment: = −(BS_AR_end − BS_AR_start) If AR goes from £40m to £52m: −(52−40) = −£12m (cash outflow)

Modelling Seasonality

Annual models smooth over seasonal patterns that can be material. A retailer that builds inventory in Q3 (for Christmas) and collects cash in Q4 has very different monthly cash flows than an annual model suggests. For businesses with significant seasonality:

  • Build a monthly model, or at minimum a quarterly model
  • Model DSO and DIO at their peak (Q3 inventory build) and trough (Q1 after Christmas sell-through)
  • Show peak cash requirement (which determines revolver sizing)
  • Show minimum cash balance (which determines covenant compliance)

The WC Normalisation Problem in M&A

Working capital at a deal closing is rarely "normal." The seller may have drawn down receivables aggressively before close (to look more cash-generative), deferred supplier payments (to inflate short-term cash), or built excessive inventory (which the buyer inherits). Normalised working capital is the WC the business needs to operate sustainably, not what it happens to have on the closing date.

Normalised WC = calculated from long-run DSO, DIO, DPO assumptions applied to LTM Revenue and COGS WC peg = Normalised WC agreed between buyer and seller WC at closing is compared to the peg: WC at close > Peg → Seller gets extra payment WC at close < Peg → Buyer deducts from purchase price Typical WC negotiation: Seller incentivised to show high WC at close (collect AR, defer AP) Buyer wants normalised WC — will scrutinise the last 3 months of BS
In a Real Bank

WC adjustments are one of the most contested elements of deal completion. The buyer's accountants and seller's accountants often disagree on what constitutes "normal" versus "managed" WC. Post-close WC disputes are common — the credit agreement will specify a mechanism (typically completion accounts or a locked-box approach) for resolving them. An analyst who models WC correctly — with proper days-based drivers and normalisation — helps the deal team negotiate from a position of knowledge rather than assumption.

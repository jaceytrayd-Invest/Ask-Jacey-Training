📌 Level 02 · Accounting · L2.06 — EBITDA Adjustments

EBITDA is the most quoted metric in corporate finance and the most abused. Understanding what legitimate adjustments look like — and how to spot manipulative ones — is a core skill for any IB analyst or credit professional.

Reported vs. Adjusted EBITDA

Companies routinely present an "adjusted" or "normalised" EBITDA that differs from the reported figure. The adjustments remove items deemed non-recurring or non-operational. In M&A, the adjusted figure determines valuation — making it the most contentious number in any deal.

Legitimate Adjustments

AdjustmentWhy legitimateHow to verify
Management fees to PE sponsorWill cease post-acquisitionConfirm in management accounts; check engagement letter termination
One-off restructuring costsGenuine one-time event — plant closure, redundancyCheck 5-year frequency — if annual, it is recurring by definition
Isolated legal settlementNon-recurring litigation with discrete causeLegal DD; verify no similar claims pending
Owner perks (personal expenses)Will not continue under new ownershipManagement accounts; interviews; tax filings

Suspect Adjustments

AdjustmentWhy suspectHow to challenge
Recurring restructuring chargesIf it happens every year, it is a cost of running the businessPull 5 years of P&L — if restructuring appears annually, include it
Cost synergies not yet achievedSpeculative — value separately in deal modelAsk for detailed plan with timeline and implementation evidence
Share-based compensationReal economic cost — dilutes shareholders or is cash-settledAssess replacement cost; include if material and recurring
The QoE Report

In any significant M&A transaction, the buyer commissions a Quality of Earnings (QoE) report from a Big 4 firm. This forensic exercise reviews every EBITDA adjustment, challenges non-recurring characterisations, and produces an adjusted EBITDA the buyer can rely on for valuation and debt sizing. The QoE is the foundation of the financial due diligence workstream.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We set our leverage covenant against a defined EBITDA written into the loan documentation. This definition specifies exactly which adjustments are permissible — and caps total add-backs at a percentage of reported EBITDA (typically 15%). This prevents EBITDA creep, where a deteriorating borrower keeps adding adjustments to avoid a covenant breach while the business actually deteriorates.

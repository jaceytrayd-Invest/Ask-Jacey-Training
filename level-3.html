Featured Lesson — Trading Comparables

📍 Valuation · Trading Comparables · L3.02

Trading comps is the first valuation method every analyst learns and the one most used in day-to-day IB work. It answers: what is this business worth relative to how the market values similar businesses today?

The Six-Step Comps Process

Step 1 — Define the peer universe

Screen for companies that are truly comparable — same industry, similar business model, overlapping customer base. Size matters but is secondary to business model similarity. Aim for 6–12 peers. Fewer than 5 and the dataset is too thin; more than 15 and you have diluted the relevance.

Key screening variables: sector and sub-sector, geography (listed on the same exchanges if possible), revenue scale (typically within ±3×), margin profile, and growth rate. Business model similarity trumps size every time.

Step 2 — Collect and normalise financials

Pull LTM (Last Twelve Months) and NTM (Next Twelve Months) financials from Bloomberg or CapIQ. For each company, collect: Revenue, Gross Profit, EBITDA, EBIT, Net Income, EPS. Normalise for any one-off items — if one peer had a large restructuring charge in the LTM period, add it back so the comparison is apples-to-apples.

LTM vs NTM

LTM (Last Twelve Months) reflects the most recent completed twelve-month period — not the fiscal year. Calculate: last fiscal year + YTD current year − same YTD prior year. NTM uses consensus analyst forecasts for the next twelve months. Use LTM as your anchor; show NTM to illustrate growth trajectory.

Step 3 — Calculate Enterprise Value and equity value for each peer

For public companies: Market Cap = Share Price × Shares Outstanding. EV = Market Cap + Net Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred − Cash. This is the EV bridge — applied to every peer in your dataset.

Step 4 — Calculate multiples

EV / Revenue = Enterprise Value ÷ LTM Revenue EV / EBITDA = Enterprise Value ÷ LTM EBITDA ← Most used EV / EBIT = Enterprise Value ÷ LTM EBIT P / E = Share Price ÷ LTM EPS ← Equity-level only EV / EBITDA(NTM) = Enterprise Value ÷ NTM EBITDA ← Forward multiple

Step 5 — Derive the implied valuation range

Take the median (and sometimes 25th–75th percentile range) of each multiple from your peer set. Apply those multiples to your subject company's financials to derive an implied EV range. Then apply the EV-to-equity bridge to get implied equity value and implied share price.

Step 6 — Sense check

Compare the implied range to the current trading price. If the stock is trading significantly below your comps-implied range, that is either an opportunity or a sign your peer set is wrong. Understand the premium or discount and be able to explain it.

The Most Common Comps Mistakes

Using gross debt instead of net debt in the EV calculation. If a company has £200m debt and £50m cash, net debt is £150m — not £200m. This error will make your peer EV wrong and your multiples wrong.

Not normalising for one-off items. If a peer had a £30m litigation settlement in the LTM period, their EBITDA is understated relative to normalised performance. Add it back — otherwise your multiple for that peer will be artificially high and skew your range.

Using the wrong share count. Always use diluted shares (including options and convertibles in the money) — not basic shares. Undercounting shares understates market cap and understates EV.

In a Real Bank

An analyst building a comps table will spend 1–2 days just sourcing and checking the data. Every figure is cross-referenced to the company's filings — not just taken from CapIQ at face value. CapIQ can and does have errors, particularly for non-standard items like pension deficits, convertible debt, or operating lease adjustments. The MD will spot a wrong figure in the client meeting — make sure every number is sourced.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We use trading comps in a different way from equity investors. Rather than looking for undervaluation, we use them to establish a floor value for the business — the price at which a distressed sale could occur if we needed to enforce security. If the comps-implied EV at a trough multiple is still well above our loan balance, we have adequate coverage. If it is not, we either pass on the deal or require additional security.

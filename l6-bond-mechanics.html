📌 Level 06 · Capital Markets · L6.04 — Bond Mechanics & Pricing

Bond mechanics are the technical foundation of all fixed income work. Understanding how bonds are priced, how yields move with prices, and what duration tells you about interest rate sensitivity is essential for DCM, credit analysis, and any role involving debt markets.

Bond Anatomy

Bond terms: Face value (par): The principal amount — typically £1,000 or $1,000 Coupon rate: Annual interest rate paid on face value (e.g., 5%) Coupon payment: Coupon rate × Face value (e.g., 5% × £1,000 = £50/year) Frequency: Semi-annual (most common) or annual Maturity: Date on which principal is repaid Issue price: Price at issuance — may be at, above, or below par Market price: Current trading price (expressed as % of par) Bond price example: "Bond trading at 95.50" = market price of 95.50% of face value = £955 for a £1,000 face value bond (trading at a discount)

Price and Yield — the inverse relationship

Bond Price = PV(Coupons) + PV(Face Value) = [C × (1 − (1+y)^-n) / y] + [F / (1+y)^n] Where: C = periodic coupon payment y = periodic yield (YTM / periods per year) n = total number of periods F = face value Key relationship: Price ↑ when yield ↓ (bond becomes more valuable) Price ↓ when yield ↑ (bond becomes less valuable) At par: Coupon rate = YTM Discount: Coupon rate < YTM (market price below par) Premium: Coupon rate > YTM (market price above par)

Duration rules of thumb: Zero-coupon bond: Duration = Maturity (all cash flow at end → maximum sensitivity) High-coupon bond: Duration < Maturity (cash flows received earlier) Longer maturity: Higher duration (further into future → more sensitive) Higher yield: Lower duration (higher discount rate → cash flows weighted toward near term) Example: 10-year, 5% coupon bond, YTM 5% (at par): Modified duration ≈ 7.7 years → If yields rise 1%: price falls approximately 7.7% → If yields fall 1%: price rises approximately 8.0% (convexity makes this asymmetric)

Convexity — why duration is an approximation

Duration gives a linear approximation of the price-yield relationship. In reality, the relationship is curved (convex). For a given yield change, the actual price change is slightly better than duration predicts for a rise in yields and slightly better than duration predicts for a fall in yields. Positive convexity is a desirable property — it means bonds perform better than the linear approximation under both rising and falling rate scenarios.

Yield Measures — Three You Must Know

Current yield: Annual coupon / Market price. Simple but ignores price appreciation/depreciation at maturity.

Yield to maturity (YTM): The internal rate of return assuming all coupons are reinvested at the YTM and the bond is held to maturity. The most complete single yield measure — standard for comparison.

Yield to worst (YTW): The lowest possible yield the bondholder could receive — assuming the issuer exercises the most unfavourable call option at the earliest available date. The most conservative yield measure — standard for callable bonds (high yield). Always used by HY investors.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

In private credit, we do not use yield to maturity in the same way public bond markets do — our loans are floating rate (SOFR/SONIA plus margin), not fixed rate bonds. Our return metric is the effective yield: the all-in floating rate plus any OID (original issue discount — a fee paid upfront that functions like additional yield). For a loan at SOFR + 500bps with 2% OID on a 5-year term: all-in yield ≈ 5.5% + 0.4% OID amortisation = 5.9% after-SOFR return before credit losses. We target 8-12%+ gross returns depending on the risk profile of the credit.

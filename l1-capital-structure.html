📌 Level 01 — Foundations · L1.03 — Capital Structure

Capital structure is how a company finances itself. The mix of debt and equity, and the terms attached to each, determines the cost of capital, the risk profile, and the returns available to every investor. Every deal involves a capital structure decision.

The Capital Structure Waterfall

In any company, capital providers are ranked by their priority of claims on cash flows and assets. Senior first, equity last. This ranking — called the waterfall — determines who gets paid, in what order, and what return they receive as compensation for their risk.

CAPITAL STRUCTURE WATERFALL (top = safest, bottom = riskiest): 1. Super Senior / Revolving Credit Facility (lowest risk, lowest return) 2. Senior Secured First Lien Loans (TLB) SOFR + 350-550bps 3. Senior Secured Second Lien Loans SOFR + 700-1000bps 4. Senior Unsecured / High Yield Bonds Fixed 7-13% 5. Subordinated / Mezzanine Debt 13-18% (PIK or cash) 6. Preferred Equity Preferred dividend 7. Common Equity Residual — unlimited upside (highest risk)

The EV Bridge — why it matters for valuation

Enterprise Value = Equity Value + Net Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred Rearranged: Equity Value = EV - Net Debt - Minority Interest - Preferred Where: Net Debt = Gross debt - Cash & equivalents Minority = Value of partially owned subsidiaries not in equity Preferred = Liquidation value of preferred equity

Modigliani-Miller — the theory

In a world without taxes, capital structure does not affect firm value (MM Proposition I). The total pie does not change — only how it is split between debt holders and equity holders. In the real world with taxes, debt creates value via the interest tax shield (interest is tax-deductible), but this benefit is offset by financial distress costs at high leverage levels.

Practical implication: there is an optimal capital structure that balances the tax shield of debt against the cost of financial distress. Investment-grade companies target low leverage (1–3× EBITDA) to preserve credit ratings. PE-backed companies use high leverage (5–7× EBITDA) to maximise equity returns while accepting higher distress risk.

Capital Structure in Deal Context

In every M&A deal, the first question after "what is it worth?" is "how do we pay for it?" The capital structure of the acquisition vehicle (how much debt, what type, at what terms) determines the deal economics for the buyer. Too much debt: the company cannot service interest → default risk. Too little: returns are diluted because you funded with expensive equity when cheap debt was available. Optimising the capital structure is a core IB skill.

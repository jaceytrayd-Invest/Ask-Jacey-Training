📌 Level 00 — Orientation · L0.01 — What Investment Banking Actually Is

Strip away the mystique. Investment banking is a precise business with a clear economic function. Understanding that function — why it exists, how it earns money, and what distinguishes it from other financial services — is the first thing a new analyst must internalise.

The Economic Function

Investment banks solve an information and access problem. Companies that need capital cannot efficiently find the investors who have it. Investors cannot efficiently assess and access opportunities across the entire market. Investment banks sit in the middle, reducing friction on both sides.

More precisely, banks perform two core functions: advisory and capital raising.

What Banks Actually Sell

Banks do not manufacture products. They sell three things: network (knowing every buyer, every investor, every potential counterparty), expertise (having done hundreds of similar transactions and knowing where deals break down), and execution capacity (being able to deploy 10–20 people on a transaction at short notice).

These three things are why a company pays a bank £5m to sell a business for £500m — rather than trying to sell it themselves. The bank's network identifies buyers the company has never spoken to. Its expertise structures the auction to maximise price. Its capacity means 15 people spend six months doing nothing but this transaction.

How Banks Earn Revenue

Advisory Fees: M&A success fee = Deal value × fee % (e.g., £800m deal × 0.75% = £6m) Retainer: small monthly fee during process (credited against success fee) Fee is only paid on CLOSE — no deal, no fee (or minimal retainer only) Underwriting Fees: ECM (IPO): gross proceeds × underwriting spread (e.g., 4% of £200m = £8m) DCM (bonds): notional × basis points (e.g., £500m × 150bps = £7.5m) Risk: underwriter may hold unsold securities on its own balance sheet
Investment Banking vs. Commercial Banking

Commercial banks hold deposits and make loans — they are on the balance sheet, taking credit risk. Investment banks primarily earn advisory and underwriting fees — they are intermediaries, taking much less balance sheet risk (though they do commit capital for underwriting). Regulatory reforms post-2008 (Dodd-Frank, Volcker Rule) pushed US banks to separate these activities further. In the UK, "ringfencing" separates retail banking from investment banking.

The Misconception to Avoid

Many students think investment banking is primarily about trading or managing money. It is not. Trading (Sales & Trading) and portfolio management (asset management) are separate businesses — sometimes within the same financial institution, but with completely different economics, cultures, and skills. IBD (Investment Banking Division) is purely advisory and capital raising. Never confuse these in an interview.

In a Real Bank

IBD analysts do not execute trades, manage portfolios, or speak to retail clients. Their days are consumed by financial modelling, pitch book preparation, data analysis, and coordinating with lawyers, accountants, and regulators on live transactions. The "glamour" is in the deal access and intellectual challenge — not in any single task, most of which are repetitive at the junior level.

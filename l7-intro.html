📌 Level 07 · Derivatives · L7.01 — Derivatives — Purpose & Taxonomy

Derivatives exist to solve a fundamental problem in financial markets: risk misallocation. Companies, investors, and institutions constantly find themselves exposed to risks they cannot bear or do not want. Derivatives allow those risks to be transferred — at a price — to those willing to take them. Understanding this core logic before any mathematics is the right starting point.

The ISDA Master Agreement

Almost all OTC derivative transactions between financial institutions are governed by an ISDA (International Swaps and Derivatives Association) Master Agreement. This is the bilateral contract that establishes netting provisions (if both parties have offsetting positions, only the net exposure is settled), default provisions, and eligible collateral. The Credit Support Annex (CSA) specifies collateral posting requirements — a key tool for managing counterparty risk in the absence of a central clearinghouse.

🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

We use derivatives primarily to manage our own portfolio risk — specifically interest rate risk. When we make a floating-rate loan, our return varies with SOFR. We sometimes enter into an interest rate swap to convert some of our floating-rate asset returns into fixed — matching the duration of our fixed obligations (if we have fixed-rate borrowings ourselves). We also use credit derivatives (CDS) to hedge specific credit concentrations in our portfolio — though we prefer structural protections (covenants, collateral) as our primary credit risk tool over derivative hedges, which carry their own counterparty risk.

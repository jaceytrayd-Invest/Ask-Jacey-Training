📌 Level 08 · Private Markets · L8.02 — The LBO — Mechanics & Value Creation

The Leveraged Buyout is the signature transaction of private equity. It is also the most analytically demanding transaction in IB — combining M&A valuation, credit analysis, operational forecasting, and exit strategy into a single integrated model. Understanding the mechanics from first principles is essential.

LBO Mechanics — sources and uses

Sources = Uses (always) SOURCES (where the money comes from): Senior Secured Debt (TLB): £Xm at SOFR + 400-550bps Junior / HY Bonds (if any): £Xm at fixed 8-12% Mezzanine (if any): £Xm at 13-18% (PIK or cash) PE Equity: £Xm (residual — 25-40% of EV) Management equity rollover: £Xm (management co-invest) Total Sources: = Purchase Price + Fees USES (where the money goes): Equity purchase price: £Xm (to existing shareholders) Refinance existing debt: £Xm (repay target's current loans) Transaction fees: £Xm (M&A advisory, legal, financing) Working capital / liquidity: £Xm (day-one operating cushion) Total Uses: = Total Sources

Three Value Creation Levers — in detail

1. EBITDA GROWTH Organic revenue growth: market share gains, pricing power, new products Margin improvement: procurement savings, operational efficiency Bolt-on acquisitions: acquire smaller companies to add revenue and synergies Example: £60m entry EBITDA → £85m exit EBITDA over 5 years 2. MULTIPLE EXPANSION Business quality transformation: software-like recurring revenue built Market re-rating: M&A multiples rose during hold period Size premium: small company acquired at 7x; sold to strategic at 9x Example: 7.0x entry → 9.0x exit on same EBITDA = 28.6% more EV 3. DEBT PAYDOWN FCF generated during hold period repays debt mechanically Each £1 of debt repaid = £1 more equity at exit Leverage effect: debt paydown amplified because starting equity was small Example: £200m debt → £160m (£40m repaid) = £40m equity gain
🔑 Lender's Perspective — JaceyTrayd

From our perspective, the LBO's three return levers are differently risk-weighted. EBITDA growth is the most operationally driven — we model it conservatively (50–70% of management's projection). Multiple expansion is market-driven — we model zero multiple expansion (exit at entry multiple) in our credit case; any expansion is upside we do not price into our analysis. Debt paydown is mechanical — it follows from the FCF schedule, and we model it conservatively (no cash sweep, mandatory amortisation only) in our base case. Our job is to ensure the deal works for us even if the PE firm's equity upside never materialises.

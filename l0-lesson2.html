📌 Level 00 — Orientation · L0.02 — Buy Side vs. Sell Side

Buy side and sell side are the most fundamental distinction in the financial industry. Every participant in capital markets sits on one side or the other. Understanding where each player sits — and what motivates them — is essential context for everything that follows.

The Core Distinction

The terms refer to which side of a securities transaction you sit on. Sell side entities sell (or facilitate the sale of) securities and advisory services. Buy side entities buy and hold securities, deploying capital on behalf of investors.

Sell SideBuy Side
WhoInvestment banks, broker-dealers, research housesAsset managers, hedge funds, PE firms, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies
Revenue modelFees (advisory, underwriting), commissions, bid-ask spreadsManagement fees (% of AUM) + performance fees (% of profits)
RoleFacilitate transactions, raise capital, provide advice and researchDeploy capital, generate investment returns for end investors
RelationshipServe buy-side clients; compete for mandatesClients of sell-side; select the banks they use for research and execution
Risk exposurePrimarily fee income; some underwriting riskDirectly exposed to investment performance — carries asset/market risk

Buy Side — the types

Long-only asset managers (Vanguard, BlackRock, Fidelity) — buy and hold diversified portfolios of stocks and bonds. Charge 0.05–1.5% management fee on AUM. Regulated; typically no leverage.

Hedge funds (Bridgewater, Citadel, Man Group) — can go long and short, use leverage and derivatives. Charge 2% management + 20% performance fee. Less regulated; high-risk, high-reward.

Private equity (Blackstone, KKR, Apax) — buy companies using leverage, improve them operationally, and sell after 3–7 years. Charge 2% management + 20% carry. Capital is illiquid (locked up).

Pension funds and sovereign wealth funds — invest on behalf of beneficiaries; long time horizons; conservative mandates; large AUM (£100bn+). They are the capital base that funds most of the activity above.

The Relationship Between the Two Sides

Banks (sell side) serve institutional investors (buy side) as clients. When an investment bank runs an M&A auction, the buy side (PE firms, strategic acquirers) are the bidders. When an investment bank prices an IPO, the buy side (asset managers) are the investors buying the shares. When an investment bank issues a bond, the buy side (bond funds, insurers) are the buyers.

Career implication

IB is sell-side. After 2–3 years as an IB analyst, many move to buy-side roles — PE, hedge funds, corporate M&A. This transition is the most common career path out of IB and the reason many analysts endure the gruelling early years. Buy-side roles typically offer more autonomy, better work-life balance (especially PE at the post-deal level), and potentially higher long-term compensation through carry.

In a Real Bank

Sell-side analysts are constantly seeking buy-side business. Research analysts publish reports hoping buy-side portfolio managers will direct their trading commissions ("soft dollars") to that bank. IBD coverage bankers maintain relationships with buy-side PE firms and strategic corporate clients hoping to win their next mandate. The sell-side relationship management machine never stops.

📌 Level 10 · Careers · L10.02 — The Interview Process — Technical & Behavioral

IB interviews are structured but not standardised. Each bank, group, and interviewer has their own emphasis. What is consistent: technical questions test a real knowledge base, behavioural questions test real character, and case studies test the ability to think under pressure. Preparing all three simultaneously — not sequentially — is the right approach.

Technical Questions — the mandatory list

These questions appear in virtually every IB first round and superday. Know them so well you can answer them accurately while maintaining eye contact and normal conversational pace.

Behavioural Questions — STAR framework

Situation: The context — when, where, what was the challenge Task: Your specific responsibility within it Action: What YOU specifically did (not "we") — step by step Result: What happened — quantified wherever possible Prepare 5 STAR stories that can flex across multiple questions: Story 1: Working under pressure / time constraint Story 2: Leadership or taking initiative Story 3: Working in a team / managing conflict Story 4: Attention to detail / catching/preventing an error Story 5: Commercial or analytical project you are proud of The same story can often answer multiple questions: "Tell me about a time you worked under pressure" = Story 1 "Tell me about a failure and what you learned" = Story 1 (different angle) "Describe a time you showed initiative" = Story 5 (different angle)
What Interviewers Are Actually Assessing

Three unspoken questions determine almost every hiring decision: Is this person smart enough? (technical questions). Will this person work hard and do what is needed? (behavioural questions). Do I want to sit next to this person at 2am during a deal crunch? (every moment of the interview). The third question is often the most decisive — and it is answered by how you handle moments of uncertainty, how you respond to pushback, and whether the interviewer enjoys talking to you. The best interview preparation includes practising how you respond when you do not know the answer — the honest, composed "I'm not certain about that specific point, but my understanding is X — am I on the right track?" is far more impressive than a confidently wrong answer.

In a Real Bank

The hiring decision is almost always made by consensus. One enthusiastic advocate can sometimes save a candidate who had one weak interview; one strong objection from an MD can veto a candidate who performed well everywhere else. The practical implication: every interaction matters — the coffee chat with the analyst before formal interviews, the elevator ride between rooms, the thank-you note sent afterwards. You are being assessed continuously, not just in the scheduled 30-minute sessions.
